Pomona's fund for children and youth programs will now draw from a fixed slice of the city's sales tax rather than a share of its general budget. Voters made the change on the June 2 primary ballot, approving Measure Z 14,250 to 6,986 — more than two to one.

The measure rewrites a promise city voters first made two years ago. In November 2024, Measure Y — the "Pomona Kids First" initiative — created the Pomona Fund for Children and Youth and set it at up to 10 percent of the city's general revenues. Measure Z keeps the 10 percent but changes where it comes from. The resolution that put it on the ballot says the fund will now take "ten percent (10%) of the City's Bradley-Burns Local Sales and Use Tax revenues to children and youth programs and services, rather than a percentage of the City's" general revenues. Bradley-Burns is the local share of the state sales tax that flows to the city.

The switch changes what the fund rides on. A cut of the general fund competes each budget year with everything else the city pays for; a dedicated share of sales-tax receipts instead rises and falls with local spending. Measure Z also restructures who runs the program: under the city attorney's impartial analysis, the fund's department would be led by a director the city hires, rather than an executive director hired by the fund's own accountability board.

Almost no organized money moved in the Measure Z campaign — with one exception, and it filed from out of town. A committee called Pomona Kids First, listing a San Francisco address, reported about $22,000 in independent spending on literature and postage against the measure — $21,987.73 across two filings, in late April and late May. Its campaign filings trace the money to a single donor. The Heising-Simons Action Fund, of Los Altos, gave the committee $125,000 on March 3 — the only itemized contribution the committee reported through mid-May, and several times what it put into the campaign against Measure Z.

The matching name is a quirk of the record. Measure Z did not create the children's fund; it changed the funding formula voters approved in 2024, and the committee that spent against Z carries the same "Pomona Kids First" name as that original measure. Its filings do not otherwise connect the two. No committee organized in favor of Measure Z: the city council placed it on the ballot itself, so its proponents were city officials, who need no campaign committee.

Measure Z was one of several Pomona questions decided June 2. Voters also settled two of the city's three contested council seats outright and sent the third, District 2, to a runoff on the November 3 ballot.
