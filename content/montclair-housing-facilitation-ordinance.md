> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents April 2026 events.*

Montclair had a state deadline bearing down on it, and on April 20 it moved fast. The city adopted what it calls a Housing Facilitation Ordinance — an urgency measure, effective immediately — reworking parts of its zoning to satisfy California's housing regulators.

The rush had a specific cause. Around March 24, Montclair received a notice of violation from the state Department of Housing and Community Development, or HCD, the agency that certifies whether a city's state-mandated housing plan complies with the law. HCD warned that the city's plan to adopt its rezoning on June 15 would push the state's finding of "substantial compliance" into the third quarter of 2026 — and it set an April 23 deadline for the city to respond.

**Why it matters:** A city's standing with HCD is not a paperwork formality. Substantial compliance is the state's stamp that a city is doing its legally required share to plan for housing; delaying or losing it invites state enforcement and puts a city on the defensive over what gets built within its own borders. By adopting the ordinance on April 20, Montclair beat its own deadline by three days.

What the ordinance actually changes comes down to four moves, each aimed at making housing easier to build:

- It permits multifamily housing "by right" — without discretionary approval — on qualifying sites where at least 20% of the units are affordable.
- It sets a minimum density of 20 units per acre, a floor that keeps projects from being built too thin to pencil out as housing.
- It requires that housing make up at least half of a mixed-use project's floor area, so "mixed-use" can't become mostly commercial with a token apartment or two.
- It directs an analysis of whether the city's height limits are themselves standing in the way of housing.

About a week later, on roughly April 29, City Manager Edward Starr and Economic Development Director Mikey Fuentes said the ordinance brings Montclair into compliance with state law, according to HeySoCal's Joe Taglieri. That is the city's own assessment. Whether HCD agrees — and lifts the cloud of the violation notice — is the question the record doesn't yet answer.
