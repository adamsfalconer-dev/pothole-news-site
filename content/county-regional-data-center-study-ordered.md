> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents an April 2026 action.*

On April 14, the Los Angeles County Board of Supervisors ordered its planners to figure out what data centers actually cost the communities around them — how much water and power they draw, what they do to rates, and how other governments rein them in. What the Board did not do was hit pause.

The motion, from Board Chair and First District Supervisor Hilda Solis, directs Regional Planning and Public Health — with Public Works, Fire and the county's sustainability office — to report back in writing within 120 days, or roughly by mid-August. A potential moratorium on new data centers was amended out before the vote. "Absent sufficient support for a full moratorium," Solis said, "the Board unanimously approved a study."

**Why it matters:** The county doesn't just weigh in on cities; it *is* the government for the unincorporated communities threaded through the East San Gabriel Valley — places like Avocado Heights, Bassett and South San Jose Hills that sit in Solis's district. And county zoning, Title 22, currently has no data-center rules at all. That gap is exactly what the study is meant to expose: right now, a data center proposed on unincorporated land would meet a rulebook that never contemplated it.

The timing is not accidental. The order landed as Monterey Park's residents were moving toward a permanent citywide ban and a valley coalition was mobilizing against zoning changes in the City of Industry. Against that backdrop, a study-not-moratorium is a deliberately middle path — enough to gather facts, not enough to stop anything while the county gathers them.
