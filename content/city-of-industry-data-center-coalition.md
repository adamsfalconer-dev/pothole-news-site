> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents events of spring 2026.*

By early May, residents from Hacienda Heights, La Puente, Walnut, Diamond Bar, West Covina, Avocado Heights and Rowland Heights had done something unusual: they built a coalition to stop a project that, on paper, does not yet exist.

There is no data-center application on file in the City of Industry. No developer has been publicly named. What there is — as the Los Angeles Times' Carlos De Loera reported on May 5, and as the local outlet Investigate LA documented over the winter — is a set of quiet zoning moves that would let data centers in, and a city with a long record of doing its business out of public view.

**Why it matters:** City of Industry has roughly 200 residents and tens of thousands of jobs; its council answers to almost no voters while making land-use decisions that land on the neighbors who surround it. When Industry rewrites its zoning, the people who breathe the air and draw on the same water table get no vote and, often, little notice. A coalition forming *before* a project is named is a bet that the only way to influence Industry is to show up early.

The concrete action is real. In February, according to the Times and Investigate LA, Industry's council unanimously rezoned the former Puente Hills Mall — the dead retail giant northwest of Azusa Avenue and the 60 Freeway — for battery storage, and adopted broader zoning changes that opponents say would permit data centers across the city. Investigate LA reported that city manager Joshua Nelson had emailed developers about allowing data centers citywide months before the zoning vote, and that a further zoning-code item sat on the March 26 agenda.

The coalition, organizing as No Data Centers SGV, folds in groups that have tangled with Industry before, from the Avocado Heights Vaquer@s to the Puente Hills Community Preservation Society. Their argument is less about any one building than about a pattern: a low-population city monetizing land and power in ways the surrounding communities absorb.

A note on language: the Times headline described "parasite" data centers, but the word came from an organizer describing the City of Industry's model, not the data centers themselves. The distinction matters, because the fight here is over a form of government as much as a land use.
