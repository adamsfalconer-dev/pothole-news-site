> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents a June 2026 dispute.*

On paper, it reads like an ordinary road project: Caltrans wants to study straightening and widening the tight S-curves on State Route 142 — Carbon Canyon Road — through Chino Hills. Read the fine print, and the fight becomes clear. The state has signaled that after the environmental review, it intends to lift the restriction that keeps large trucks off the canyon — whether or not the widening is ever built.

That restriction was not easily won. The ban on four-plus-axle trucks took effect in May 2024, the payoff of more than seven years of pressure from the city and its residents. Caltrans's proposal, city officials say, would undo it.

**Why it matters:** For the neighborhoods along Carbon Canyon, SR-142 is not one route among many. As one resident put it at a June hearing, it is their "one and only" way in and out. Putting big rigs back on a road of blind S-curves that doubles as a neighborhood's only lifeline is the kind of change residents feel every day — and the reason the city is talking about court, not just comment letters.

Chino Hills is not waiting quietly. In a June 11 statement, the city said it opposes the removal, has submitted a formal comment letter, has retained the transportation-engineering firm Iteris to analyze the proposal, and is prepared to legally challenge any decision to lift the ban. Mayor Brian Johsz is on record against it, and a state senator has written to the Caltrans district director. More than 80 residents watched the June 10 virtual hearing from a city-run viewing site.

One point of precision: the city's opposition has come through press releases and its comment letter, not a formal council vote. That distinction matters for what happens next — a resolution would put the council on record and could authorize litigation.
