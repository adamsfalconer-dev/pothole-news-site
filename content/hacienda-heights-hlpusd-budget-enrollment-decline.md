> *Reported in July 2026 as part of Pothole News's founding archive; it documents the district's June 25, 2026 budget adoption.*

Hacienda La Puente Unified is planning for a smaller future — and its 2026-27 budget shows what that starts to cost. On June 25, its board adopted a roughly $354 million spending plan built on the assumption that enrollment will keep sliding, from 15,184 students last year to a projected 14,123 by 2028-29, a loss of more than 1,000 children in three years. To match spending to that shrinking student body, the budget builds in savings from a planned reduction of 25 certificated positions — teachers and other credentialed staff — which the district says it intends to reach "through attrition and retirements where feasible."

At the same time, the district is not in fiscal distress. The budget carries a "positive certification," the state's healthiest designation, meaning Hacienda La Puente projects it can meet its financial obligations this year and the two that follow. It ends the year with a projected fund balance of about $144 million and holds its reserve for economic uncertainties at the state-required 3% minimum.

In California, money follows students. Districts are funded largely on how many children show up, so shrinking enrollment is a slow squeeze on revenue — and eventually on staffing. Hacienda La Puente's funded average daily attendance, the count its state money is based on, has fallen from 17,133 in 2021-22 to a projected 13,916 by 2028-29 — a drop of roughly 3,200, or about 19%, in seven years. The district attributes the slide to "demographic and migration factors common across the San Gabriel Valley."

The budget spends more than it takes in this year — but not because it is in the red. Hacienda La Puente projects about $313.2 million in general-fund revenue, most of it ($221.7 million) from the state's per-student funding formula, known as LCFF, plus $70 million in other state money, $13.3 million in federal funds and $8.2 million from local sources. Planned spending totals about $353.9 million, led by $137.8 million in teacher and other certificated salaries, $53.7 million for classified staff and $74.9 million in employee benefits.

Most of the roughly $42 million the district plans to draw from its accumulated fund balance this year is one-time grant money — pandemic-era learning-recovery, arts and mental-health funds that are winding down — being spent as designed. But about $15 million of it is what the state calls "unrestricted deficit spending": day-to-day money going out faster than it comes in. The district reports that figure openly in its budget, notes that it stays within the state's tolerance, and ties it partly to enrollment-driven revenue growing more slowly than costs. To hold its reserve steady, the board adopted a Fiscal Stabilization Plan last November built on staffing reductions keyed to enrollment — the plan the 25-position reduction belongs to.

One number in the budget is already out of date, in the district's favor. The plan was built on a state cost-of-living adjustment of 2.87% — the figure in the governor's proposal — because California had not yet passed its own budget when Hacienda La Puente adopted its. The state budget the governor signed on June 29 set that adjustment higher, at 4.31%, which the district had deliberately left out as "pending." That means the adopted budget likely understates the state money coming in, not overstates it.

Hacienda La Puente is not alone. Declining enrollment is reshaping school budgets across the San Gabriel Valley — the regional slide the district itself points to, in its own budget, to explain why its funded attendance keeps dropping. The Hacienda La Puente budget was adopted by a board led by President Gino Kwok, with Vice President Adriana Quiñónes, Clerk Nancy Loera and members Jeffrey De La Torre and Stephanie Serrano.
