> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents June 2026 events.*

Hacienda La Puente Unified is planning for a smaller future. On June 25, its board adopted a 2026-27 budget built on the assumption that enrollment will keep sliding — from 15,184 students last year to a projected 14,123 by 2028-29, a loss of more than 1,000 children in three years.

Even so, the district is not in financial trouble. The budget carries a "positive certification," the state's healthiest designation, meaning Hacienda La Puente projects it can meet its financial obligations this year and the two that follow.

**Why it matters:** In California, money follows students. Districts are funded largely on how many children show up, so shrinking enrollment is a slow squeeze on revenue. Hacienda La Puente's funded average daily attendance has fallen from 17,133 in 2021-22 to a projected 13,916 by 2028-29 — a drop of roughly 3,200, or about 19%, in seven years. The district attributes the slide to "demographic and migration factors common across the San Gabriel Valley."

The budget numbers show a district living within its means while it adjusts. For 2026-27, Hacienda La Puente projects about $313.2 million in general-fund revenue — most of it, $221.7 million, from the state's per-student funding formula, known as LCFF, plus $70 million in other state money, $13.3 million in federal funds and $8.2 million from local sources. Planned spending totals about $353.9 million, led by $137.8 million in teacher and other certificated salaries, $53.7 million for classified staff and $74.9 million in employee benefits. The plan is built on a 2.87% state cost-of-living adjustment.

Spending exceeds revenue by roughly $41 million on paper — but that is not an operating deficit, and it should not be read as one. The budget still projects a positive ending balance, and the gap reflects capital outlay plus the planned spend-down of one-time and restricted funds the district set aside in prior years, not day-to-day costs outrunning income. The positive certification is the proof: the district is drawing down money it had already banked, by design.

Hacienda La Puente is not alone. Declining enrollment is reshaping school budgets across the San Gabriel Valley — the same regional pressure behind Pomona Unified's decision, which this newsroom documented, to close Armstrong Elementary in Diamond Bar and consolidate its students. The budget was adopted by a board led by President Gino Kwok, with Vice President Adriana Quiñónes, Clerk Nancy Loera and members Jeffrey De La Torre and Stephanie Serrano.
