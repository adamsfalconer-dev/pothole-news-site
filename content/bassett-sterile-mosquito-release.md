> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents an event of early July 2026.*

Every week since July 7, roughly 30,000 mosquitoes have been let loose from cardboard tubes over a 25-acre patch of Bassett — and the local vector-control district is hoping they will win.

The insects are all male, all sterile, and all non-biting. The San Gabriel Valley Mosquito & Vector Control District has raised them to carry a naturally occurring bacterium, Wolbachia, that scrambles reproduction: when one of these lab males mates with a wild *Aedes aegypti* female, her eggs never hatch. Do that often enough, across enough generations, and the biting population folds in on itself. The district's slogan puts it plainly — "when they MATCH, their eggs won't HATCH."

**Why it matters:** *Aedes aegypti* is not a native nuisance; it is an invasive, day-biting mosquito that can carry dengue, Zika and chikungunya, and it has proven stubbornly hard to knock down with sprays alone. A tool that suppresses the species without blanketing neighborhoods in insecticide is the kind of thing that, if it works here, gets copied across Southern California. This is the district testing whether it does.

The program, branded SGV Mosquito Match, is the district's first deployment of the Sterile Insect Technique. It runs 16 weeks, July through October, with releases twice a week. The males are EPA-approved WB1 mosquitoes; because only females bite, releasing males adds no bites and no disease risk. Officials picked the Bassett zone — bounded by East Temple Avenue, Millbury Avenue, Moccasin Street and Vineland Avenue — off surveillance data showing an entrenched *Aedes* population, the sort of place where a controlled trial can actually be measured.

"SGV Mosquito Match provides an innovative approach that utilizes mosquitoes of the same species to work for us, not against us," District Manager Jason Farned said in announcing the launch. The district's own promise is modest and honest about time: biting pressure, it says, will ease "gradually" over the season, not overnight.

A word on the numbers, because they traveled badly. Several outlets — and plenty of social posts — described "500,000 mosquitoes" being released. That figure is the rough program total across all 16 weeks, not a weekly count; the district's weekly release is about 30,000. It is a big number either way, but the distinction is the difference between a swarm and a science experiment.

Whether it works is a genuinely open question, and the district has been careful not to oversell it. The measure of success is not zero mosquitoes; it is a documented drop in the wild *Aedes* count inside the zone by fall, enough to justify doing it somewhere larger next year.
