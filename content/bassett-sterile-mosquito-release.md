> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents a program that began July 7, 2026.*

Every week since July 7, roughly 30,000 mosquitoes have been let loose over a 25-acre patch of Bassett — and the local vector-control district is hoping they will win.

The insects are all male, all sterile, and all non-biting. The San Gabriel Valley Mosquito & Vector Control District has raised them to carry a naturally occurring bacterium, Wolbachia, that scrambles reproduction: when one of these lab males mates with a wild Aedes aegypti female, her eggs never hatch. Aedes aegypti is an invasive, day-biting mosquito that can carry dengue, Zika and chikungunya, and it has proven stubbornly hard to knock down with sprays alone — a tool that suppresses it without blanketing neighborhoods in insecticide is the kind of thing that, if it works here, gets copied elsewhere in Southern California.

The program, branded SGV Mosquito Match, is the district's first deployment of the Sterile Insect Technique. It runs 16 weeks, July through October, using EPA-approved WB1 mosquitoes. Because only females bite, releasing males adds no bites and no disease risk. Officials picked the Bassett zone — bounded by East Temple Avenue, Millbury Avenue, Moccasin Street and Vineland Avenue — off surveillance data showing an entrenched Aedes population.

"SGV Mosquito Match provides an innovative approach that utilizes mosquitoes of the same species to work for us, not against us," District Manager Jason Farned said in announcing the launch.

A word on the numbers, because they traveled badly: some outside coverage described "500,000 mosquitoes" being released. That figure, where it appears, is the rough program total across all 16 weeks — not a weekly count. The district's own released weekly figure is about 30,000.
