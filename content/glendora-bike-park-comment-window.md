Glendora residents have through Friday, July 31, to comment — for a second time — on the city's plan to build a bike park and a new trail network in the South Hills Wilderness.

The project would put two asphalt pump tracks, three progressive jump trails, two downhill mountain-bike trails closed to hikers and horses, a climbing trail and a roughly five-mile cross-country loop on South Hills Park — the 318-acre city-owned park between Glendora Avenue and the Metro A Line tracks — along with a trailhead kiosk, a restroom, parking and a regraded access road off Glendora Avenue. Construction would disturb about 4.25 acres of the site and take about 11 months once it begins, according to the city's environmental study.

The city first circulated that study for public review from Dec. 11 to Jan. 9. Then two state agencies weighed in. The Department of Fish and Wildlife raised Crotch's bumble bee, bats, special-status reptiles and stream features on the site; the Department of Parks and Recreation flagged the coastal California gnatcatcher — a protected songbird the study says was observed on site — and asked whether bald or golden eagles nest within 660 feet of the work. The city says it agreed with Fish and Wildlife's recommended mitigation measures and revised the study's biological-resources analysis — a revision substantial enough that, under state CEQA guidelines, the document had to be recirculated with a fresh comment window. The revisions are marked in the new draft, and the city asks reviewers to focus their comments on them.

State Parks also told the city that because the parkland sits within a federal Land and Water Conservation Fund boundary, no construction or grading there can begin until the National Park Service issues its grant award notice and federal consultations are complete, according to the department's letter.

Comments go to Kelsey Fay, senior management analyst in the Recreation and Human Services Department — by mail at 116 E. Foothill Blvd., Glendora, CA 91741, or by email at kfay@cityofglendora.gov, with questions at (626) 914-8228. Printed copies of the study are available weekdays 8 a.m. to 5 p.m. at the city clerk's office at City Hall and at the Glendora Public Library, 140 Glendora Ave.

The city is both the applicant and the approving agency here: the park is city land and the bike park is the city's own project. The review lists what comes next — adopting the environmental document and approving the project — but the notice does not say which city body will act, or when.
