Pomona's city council decides Monday whether to hand up to five years of citywide tree work to Mariposa Tree Management — over the written objections of two companies that bid against it.

Staff recommends Mariposa, whose five-year price came in at $4,444,076. The contract would run three years, backdated to July 1, with two one-year options. The city has budgeted $1,499,680 for tree work this fiscal year; Mariposa's first-year cost would be $962,899.

The backdating has a reason. The city's agreement with West Coast Arborists for the same work — approved by the council in September 2021, then extended twice by six months while the city tried to rebid the job — expired June 30. Staff asked that Mariposa's contract begin the following day, according to its report, "to ensure continuity of service."

Two of the six responsive bidders filed formal protests. Staff is standing by its recommendation anyway. The item sits on Monday's discussion calendar rather than the consent list, meaning the council takes it up in the open rather than approving it in a block.

Golden West Arbor Services protested on price. Its five-year bid was $2,917,669 — about $1.5 million under Mariposa's. On July 9, operations manager Johnathan J. Orozco asked the city to "reconsider your decision on not accepting our qualified bid submittal." His letter pointed to the gap: "We are one of the top three lowest bidders."

The city's answer, six days later, was that price was never the test. The solicitation "was conducted as a qualifications-based Request for Proposals (RFP), not a low-bid procurement," acting purchasing manager Sonia Gil wrote. "While price was considered as part of the evaluation and assessed for reasonableness, it was only one of several evaluation criteria and was not the sole basis for award." Golden West scored lower, she wrote, because its municipal projects "appeared to be significantly smaller in scope" and parts of its proposal "referenced the County and appeared to have been prepared for a county solicitation rather than being specifically tailored to the City of Pomona's RFP."

That method matters to the arithmetic. Ranked on five-year price alone, Mariposa's bid was the fourth of the six the city deemed responsive; Golden West, North Star and Tiger Tree all bid less. Ranked on the city's 240-point scale, Mariposa finished first with 211.20.

West Coast Arborists, which came second at 200.01 points and bid $4,514,609, protested on process. Its president, Patrick Mahoney, wrote on July 1 that the company "was never afforded the opportunity to participate in any interview, negotiations, clarification process, or BAFO" — a best-and-final offer — "despite the relatively small pricing difference between WCA and the recommended proposer." That difference is 1.6%.

Mahoney also wrote that "several significant unit prices submitted by Mariposa appear to be identical to WCA's proposal, while numerous additional line items are only marginally lower." He was explicit that he was not alleging misconduct: "Although WCA is not suggesting any impropriety, these similarities raise legitimate questions regarding whether post-proposal pricing discussions or negotiations occurred and, if so, whether all finalists were afforded the same opportunity." He asked the city to hold the award until the procurement records could be reviewed.

The city's response addressed the resemblance directly. Prices "were not opened or reviewed until after the evaluation and scoring of the technical qualifications was completed," Gil wrote — and Mariposa had obtained the pricing legally, through public-records requests. "Mariposa submitted PRA's for all WCA agreements with the City and may have used this information to submit its bid, resulting in prices similar to WCA bid prices." On the missing interview, she cited a clause of the city's own RFP reserving the right to pick a single finalist without further discussion. The protest, she wrote, "is without merit."

Monday is not the council's first pass at this work. Staff's report says the council threw out an earlier round of bidding in December on the city attorney's advice and started over as a request for proposals — the process that produced Monday's recommendation, and both protests.
