Glendora's City Council holds a public hearing at 7 tonight on maximum trash and recycling rates for the next five years — and under the city's notice, written protests count only if the city clerk has them before the hearing closes.

The resolution before the council would authorize maximum rates for solid-waste collection and recycling from July 15, 2026, through July 14, 2031, under the city's franchise with Athens Services. Beginning July 15 and on each July 1 after that through 2030, the maximum rates could rise by the consumer price index for trash and garbage collection plus 1 percent — increases the city attributes to added services under the franchise, including the costs of state-mandated organics diversion programs. The proposed schedule lists a single-home rate of $52.71 in total — $47.49 for service plus an 11 percent city fee.

That first step does not reach residential customers. The city says residential service itself does not change: homes already use the three-cart system, and the notice states residential rates stay at the July 1, 2026 adjustment, with the next one not due until July 1, 2027. The notice lists only proposed maximums; it does not print what customers pay today. Businesses and apartment buildings see more. Under the notice, Athens adds a $23.05 supplemental fee annually to their net rates, starting July 15 and each following July 1 through 2030, and they must move to the three-container system — trash, recycling and organics — to comply with the state's SB 1383 organics law.

Protests must be written; email and fax submissions are not accepted. They must reach the city clerk at 116 E. Foothill Blvd., by mail or in person, before the close of tonight's hearing — a postmark does not qualify, which means mailing one now is already too late. Walking it in is the only route left. One protest counts per parcel, from the property owner or the customer who pays the bill, and it needs the protester's name and signature, the property address or parcel number, and a clear statement of opposition. If the owners of a majority of the affected parcels protest, the rates cannot be adopted.
