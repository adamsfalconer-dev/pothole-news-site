La Verne's City Council is set Monday to consider more than doubling the dedicated fee that pays for the city's paramedics — and to decide the question through a property-owner assessment vote rather than putting it to the general electorate in November.

The item, No. 13 on the July 20 agenda, is a discussion item, not a public hearing: staff is asking the council to "direct staff to proceed with a Proposition 218 assessment process" to raise the paramedic service fee — the charge city voters first approved in 1980 as Measure GG — to $14 a month, with a yearly adjustment tied 80 percent to a fire-personnel cost index and 20 percent to a medical-care inflation index. The city's Measure GG page lists the current charge at $6.90 a month, collected on residents' water and refuse bills.

The gap staff is trying to close is large. According to the Fire Department's report, Measure GG brings in about $1,059,840 a year, and billing insurers for ambulance and emergency care adds roughly $1.7 million more — against a cost of running paramedic and ambulance transport service the report puts at about $3.8 million — a figure it calls outdated and likely low, and says could run to more than $4 million. The rest is covered by the city's general fund. Emergency medical calls now make up about 74 percent of the Fire Department's calls for service, the report says. At $14 a month, the fee would generate about $2,150,400 a year.

The bigger change may be who gets to decide. City staff is recommending the increase move "through a Proposition 218 process versus placing the item on the November General Election ballot," because the city attorney has determined the charge is a property assessment under Article XIII D — the part of state law, added by Proposition 218, that governs how such charges are approved. That means it would be decided not by registered voters but by the owners of the affected parcels, through a mailed ballot weighted by each parcel's share of the cost — and the increase fails if the opposing ballots outweigh those in favor. It would not appear on the November 3 ballot, which for La Verne carries City Council seats.

For residents, Monday is a starting point, not a decision on the fee itself, and it carries no formal protest right. If the council directs staff to proceed, the city would prepare a fee study and parcel list, then mail every affected property owner a notice and a ballot at least 45 days before a public hearing at which the ballots are counted. None of those dates has been set. A resident who wants to weigh in Monday can email the city clerk at cityclerk@LaVerneCA.gov by noon that day, speak at the meeting in person, or join by Zoom at LaVerneCA.gov/zoom; comments are limited to three minutes.
