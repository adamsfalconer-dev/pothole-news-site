> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents the March 2026 censure and the sanctions it imposed.*

The South El Monte City Council formally censured Mayor Gloria Olmos on March 3 and suspended her access to public funds for the remainder of her term.

The council acted on an outside investigation. It had retained JL Group, LLC, which examined three allegations against Olmos and sustained one — that she "engaged in a conversation and/or forwarded information to an outside third-party regarding an item that had been placed on the closed session agenda of a special city council meeting scheduled for June 26, 2025," according to the censure resolution's findings. The other two were not sustained — the staff report says there was insufficient evidence to either prove or disprove them.

Olmos denied it. The minutes record that she told the council she had not violated the Brown Act, that the report was inaccurate, that she would not put the city at jeopardy, that she wanted to finish reading the report — and she asked the council not to censure her.

A censure is the council's own act. The resolution records what the council concluded after its investigator reported, not what a court has decided. On that basis the council found Olmos "engaged in violations of the Brown Act supporting the City Council's position of a formal censure," and voted to formally censure her.

The hearing was hers to open. Olmos introduced the item, was given 10 minutes to speak, announced her recusal, and opened the floor. Nine people spoke. The minutes record four of them — among them a teacher and two other community members — speaking in her support; one urging the council to take no action; two asking the city to explain what she was accused of; and two others raising concerns about the underlying conduct without asking for any particular outcome.

Councilmember Acosta then moved to take no action. No one seconded him and the motion failed. Delgado moved to adopt the censure, Mayor Pro Tem Bojorquez seconded, and it carried 3 to 1 with one abstention — Bojorquez, Rodriguez and Delgado in favor, Acosta against, Olmos abstaining. Neither the resolutions nor the minutes record Acosta's reasons.

A second resolution set the price. It suspends reimbursement for conference attendance and related travel and lodging expenses, and access to public funds, for the remainder of the Mayor's term of office, and asks her to retake AB 1234 ethics training. The council imposed the sanctions, in the resolution's own words, "to the extent such sanctions are permissible or enforceable by applicable law," and found they "will not interfere with any rights given to her by virtue of her elected office."

The draft resolution in the March 3 packet carried four sanctions; the version the council adopted carries two. Missing from the final text: removal from her office space and from her committee assignments. Added, and absent from staff's own list of sanctions "for consideration": the suspension of access to public funds. No record reached shows when or by whom that change was made. The phrase appears once elsewhere in the packet, in the staff report's list of sanctions "commonly imposed" by other government bodies "that have been upheld" — among them "revocation of special privileges, such as suspending official travel or access to public funds." The city's municipal code does not use the phrase anywhere. Its one provision touching a mayor's formal authority over money, Section 2.16.020, requires the mayor's signature on "all warrants drawn on the treasury" before the treasurer may pay money out — and no document reached here says whether the sanction reaches that duty.

Olmos signed both resolutions. The city's code requires it: the mayor "shall sign all ordinances and resolutions adopted by the council during his or her presence." Below the signature block on each, in handwriting, she wrote: "I Don't agree."
