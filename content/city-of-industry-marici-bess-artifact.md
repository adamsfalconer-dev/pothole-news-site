The City of Industry's council approved a roughly 400-megawatt battery storage plant on Gale Avenue in February. Five months later, the city has published no minutes, no recording and no record of how any council member voted.

That the vote happened at all is documented in two places, neither of them a record of the vote. One is a state environmental filing — a two-page form stating the city "approved the above described project on 02/12/2026." The other is a staff report the city wrote to itself two weeks later, which describes the council approving the plant "at the same February 12, 2026 meeting." The city has posted minutes for exactly one of its 14 council meetings this year, a January special meeting — not the February vote.

State law does not require it to post any of them. The Government Code tells the council to have its clerk "keep a correct record of its proceedings," and tells the clerk to "keep an accurate record of the proceeding of the legislative body." Both are duties to write things down. Neither sets a deadline, requires publication, or mentions recordings. Industry is not breaking the law by leaving the record of a 400-megawatt approval unpublished for five months. It is choosing to. The city has published no explanation, and none of its 2026 agendas takes up the lag.

The project, from Marici Project LLC, would put 480 battery containers and 120 transformer containers at 16207–16253 Gale Ave., connecting to Southern California Edison's Walnut substation by a new 220-kilovolt overhead line. It would be operated by Aypa Power, which is owned by the private equity firm Blackstone, LAist reported in May in the first account of the project. The council approved it on a mitigated negative declaration — the lighter form of environmental review, which skips a full environmental impact report.

The money runs through a community benefit agreement, which Industry's zoning code requires before any battery plant can be permitted in its industrial zone. The version in the council's packet is a draft; the city has not published the signed one. In it, the developer "shall make a one-time cash payment to the City in the amount of Two Million Dollars" within 30 days of the first building permit, and a second payment of $4 million after the city's final inspections and before it issues the certificate of occupancy, which the agreement says the city "shall issue within two (2) business days." The agreement also anticipates "at least Three Million Dollars" in sales and use tax revenue; if the project falls short, the city invoices the developer for the difference. The deal runs until the plant is decommissioned. The environmental review anticipates a 35-year operating life, after which the company may decommission the plant or seek to extend its permit.

Neighbors are fighting it. The Puente Hills Community Preservation Association, a residents' group, says its petition drive began in March and has gathered more than 3,500 signatures, and that it has sued the city to demand full environmental review. The case — Puente Hills Community Preservation Association, et al. v. City of Industry, et al. — appeared in closed session on three council agendas between late March and May 28, and has not been listed since. The council's April 28 special-meeting agenda listed, in open session, a "Joint Defense and Confidentiality Agreement between the City and Marici Project LLC." Whether the council approved that agreement is, again, absent from any published record.

Marici Project LLC has not publicly commented on the lawsuit; LAist reported in May that Aypa Power did not return its request for comment.
