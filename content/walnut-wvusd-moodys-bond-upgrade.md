> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents the April and May 2026 bond sale.*

Walnut Valley Unified has refinanced a decade-old block of school debt at a lower interest rate, and says the savings go to the property owners who repay it on their tax bills.

The district says the deal will deliver "more than $1.8 million in taxpayer relief to the community over the next 15 years," and that the schools keep none of it. "Under State law, all savings from the GO Bond refinancings will go directly to the taxpayers," its announcement says. "The District will not receive any additional funds."

Moody's rated the new bonds Aaa, its top grade, on April 15 — the same day the school board authorized the sale. The bonds went out eight days later in a competitive sale, which Loop Capital Markets won with the low bid, and the district reported a true interest cost of 2.77% to the state. The deal closed May 7.

The new issue is smaller than the debt it retires: $23.7 million of new bonds against $25.8 million of old ones. That works because investors paid a premium — $26.3 million for $23.7 million of face value — and the extra covers the gap.

The board had made the sale conditional before any of it happened. Resolution 26-19 required that the refinancing produce net present value savings of "not less than 4.00% of the par amount of the Refunded Bonds." The bonds were issued, so the deal cleared that floor.

What is being refinanced is itself a refinancing. The 2016 bonds — $40.7 million when they were issued — rolled up school bonds that voters approved as far back as 2000. They first become callable this summer.
