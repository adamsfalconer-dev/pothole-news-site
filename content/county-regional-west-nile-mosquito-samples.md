> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account covers West Nile detections since late June 2026 and was updated to the current record as of July 14.*

Five mosquito samples have tested positive for West Nile virus in the San Gabriel Valley this year, and the count hasn't moved in nearly a week. The San Gabriel Valley Mosquito and Vector Control District's tally, last updated July 13, still reads the same as it did July 8: one positive sample each in Alhambra, Rosemead, West Covina, Baldwin Park and La Puente.

Alhambra was first, on June 23 — the district's first positive sample of the year. Rosemead followed June 30, then West Covina, Baldwin Park and La Puente all turned up positive the same day, July 8. No West Nile-infected dead birds have been reported, the other signal the district watches for.

West Nile is endemic to the area and keeps coming back every year, said Tristan Hallum, the district's director of scientific programs. "There is no cure or human vaccine for WNV," he said. "The key to staying safe and protecting your health is to prevent mosquito bites." The virus spreads locally through the southern house mosquito, Culex quinquefasciatus, a common species that bites at night.

That's a different insect from the one behind the district's other high-profile mosquito effort this summer. Since July 7, the district has been releasing sterile male mosquitoes over a patch of Bassett as part of a program called SGV Mosquito Match — but that program targets an invasive day-biter, Aedes aegypti, not the night-biting Culex that carries West Nile. The district says the two programs don't overlap: even where the sterile-release zone is working, residents there still need the same West Nile precautions as everyone else.

The marker to watch has been whether a human case would show up inside the district's own footprint — and this week, one turned up elsewhere in Los Angeles County instead. The county's health department confirmed its first human West Nile case of the season on July 13: an Antelope Valley resident hospitalized with West Nile encephalitis after symptoms began in late June. "The first human cases of West Nile virus are an important reminder that we all need to take steps to prevent mosquito bites and mosquito breeding," said Dr. Muntu Davis, the county's health officer. The Antelope Valley — high desert on the far side of the San Gabriel Mountains — has its own separate vector-control agency, outside the San Gabriel Valley district's territory. Long Beach, which also runs its own health department, reported California's first symptomatic human case of the year in late June; that patient was hospitalized and is now recovering at home. As of July 14, no human case has been confirmed inside the San Gabriel Valley district's own service area, which includes Pasadena.

The district's guidance is unchanged: tip out standing water, toss containers that collect it, and protect yourself with a repellent containing an EPA-registered ingredient such as DEET, picaridin or oil of lemon eucalyptus.
