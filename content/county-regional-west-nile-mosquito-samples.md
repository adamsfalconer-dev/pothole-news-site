> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents June and July 2026 events.*

West Nile virus is circulating in San Gabriel Valley mosquitoes again. Since late June, the San Gabriel Valley Mosquito & Vector Control District has detected the virus in five mosquito samples across five cities — Alhambra, Rosemead, West Covina, Baldwin Park and La Puente.

The first turned up June 23 in Alhambra, the district's first positive sample of the year, followed by Rosemead on June 30 and West Covina, Baldwin Park and La Puente all on July 8. No West Nile-infected dead birds — another signal the district watches for — have been reported.

**Why it matters:** West Nile is endemic to the area — it returns every year — and there is no human vaccine and no cure, said Tristan Hallum, the district's director of scientific programs. The risk to people climbs from June through October. The virus is carried mainly by the southern house mosquito, *Culex quinquefasciatus*, a common local species that bites at night.

The district's advice comes as a three-word slogan, "Tip, Toss, Protect": dump standing water at least once a week, toss out unused containers that collect it, and use an insect repellent recommended by the EPA or CDC.

The year's first human cases in the broader region have landed close by, but outside the district's territory. Long Beach reported California's first symptomatic human case of 2026 around June 26, and Los Angeles County's first human case was in the Antelope Valley, according to NBC Los Angeles. Both are outside the San Gabriel Valley district's service area.

One point worth keeping straight: this is not the district's much-publicized "SGV Mosquito Match," the summer experiment releasing sterile male mosquitoes in nearby Bassett since July 7. That program targets invasive *Aedes* mosquitoes — the aggressive, day-biting *Aedes aegypti* and *albopictus*. West Nile is spread by an entirely different insect, the night-active *Culex*. Same district and the same high-tech toolkit, but two different mosquitoes and two different problems: the sterile-release program does nothing to the *Culex* that carry West Nile.
