> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents April 2026 events.*

More than 50 City of La Verne workers — the employees who keep the city's utilities and everyday services running — have joined the Teamsters, the union said in an announcement on April 13.

Teamsters Local 1932, the Inland Empire local, said the workers voted to come aboard, adding a newly organized unit inside a small city's workforce.

**Why it matters:** A new bargaining unit changes who sits across the table from La Verne at contract time, and it is another marker of a labor-organizing push moving through the region's smaller cities — the kind of municipal workforces that often go unorganized. Who represents the people who run a city's water, streets and public services shapes what those services cost and how they are staffed.

The city's own records back up the core of the union's account. Minutes from the May 4 La Verne City Council meeting list a "La Verne Miscellaneous/General Unit (Teamsters)" among the city's recognized employee organizations — a sign the city now deals with the Teamsters as the representative for that group of workers.

Several specifics, though, are not yet nailed down in the public record. The exact number of workers beyond "more than 50," the precise name and job classifications of the unit, and how the workers actually won recognition — an employee election, a card-check agreement, or a council action — aren't established by the documents available. The Teamsters describe workers voting to join; the city's minutes confirm a recognized Teamsters unit without spelling out how it got there.

The union's announcement quoted its secretary-treasurer, Randy Korgan, and one of the newly organized employees, Eugene Perez, a City of La Verne utility worker.
