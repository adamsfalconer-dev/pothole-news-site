The Chino City Council holds its last regular meeting before September tonight, and much of the agenda is what people pay the city: public hearings on new fees and this year's landscape assessments, plus a vote on sending a new business tax to the November ballot.

Most city fees already rose July 1 under Chino's annual inflation adjustment, according to the staff report; tonight's hearing on amending the master fee schedule covers what that process doesn't. New fees would take effect Aug. 1, including affordable-housing compliance monitoring charges of $458 to $2,554 depending on project size, a $500 backflow plan check, a $2,500 certificate of compliance and an $87 water-meter setup charge.

The bigger swings hit special events starting Sept. 1. Permits for small events — 200 people or fewer — would drop from $1,467 to $1,002, while permits for the largest events, over 1,500 people, would rise from $1,961 to $2,880. Nonprofits putting on those major events, currently charged nothing, would pay $1,152; events co-sponsored by the city, now $3,320, would pay nothing. Parks and recreation fees mostly hold: staff counts eight increases and six new charges across a schedule of more than 150 lines, including higher batting-cage rates and a new $1-a-minute late-pickup fee.

The second hearing sets 2026-27 rates in the city's landscape and lighting maintenance districts. Staff proposes increases in 107 of 192 zones and no decreases: every lot in District 2002-1 would pay about 3.24% more under an inflation adjustment, while 33 zones in the older districts rise as much as 5.01% — the largest percentage increase takes an annual assessment from $46.67 to $49.01, and the biggest dollar jump adds $96.60 a year in District 2002-1. Per-lot assessments across the six districts range from $8.51 to $3,074.18. Together they would raise about $710,000 toward $1.44 million in maintenance costs, with the city covering the rest — $730,478, up from $588,882. The staff report says no proposed rate exceeds its zone's authorized maximum — many zones are already at it and stay flat — so no property-owner ballot election is required; owners can protest in writing or at the hearing.

Also on the agenda: a resolution calling a Nov. 3 ballot measure that would replace the business-license tax Chino adopted in 1987 — a structure staff says caps gross-receipts-based license taxes at $1,250 a year. Under the proposed ordinance, businesses grossing $75,000 or less would pay a flat $25 — about 56% of the city's roughly 4,700 active businesses, staff estimates — and larger ones would pay $25 plus 60 cents to $1.50 per $1,000 of gross receipts above $75,000, depending on category. Staff projects an additional $4.8 million a year. As a general tax it would need a simple majority in November; the item also asks the council to authorize the mayor to file the ballot argument in favor and to set aside $100,000 for election costs and voter information.

On the consent calendar, Chino Valley Unified would pay the city $1.13 million for school policing next school year — five school resource officers and a sergeant covering campuses including Chino, Don Lugo and Buena Vista high schools.

To weigh in tonight: speakers fill out a Request to Speak form at the chamber entrance, and phone comments run through (909) 340-4280, with lines opening 30 minutes before the meeting. Emailed comments go to CityClerk@CityofChino.org with the meeting date and item number, due two hours before the meeting starts — the agenda doesn't say whether that clock runs from the 4:30 p.m. closed session or the 6 p.m. open session, so email by 2:30 to be certain. The open session starts at 6 at City Hall, 13220 Central Ave.
