> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents a July 9, 2026 sentencing.*

After the Eaton Fire, a couple who had lost their home applied to rent a property listed by real estate agent Mike Kobeissi. After they submitted the application, they were told the rent was 38 percent higher than what had been advertised. They declined the listing and reported it to the state attorney general's office. On July 9, Kobeissi was sentenced over it.

The case was prosecuted by California attorney general Rob Bonta, not the Los Angeles County District Attorney. And Kobeissi was convicted not of price gouging but of false advertising, a misdemeanor under Business and Professions Code Section 17500 — a distinction his defense stressed publicly. California's price-gouging statute, which limits how much prices can rise after a declared emergency, was not the law he was found guilty of breaking.

Kobeissi's attorney, Dale Galipo, called it "an unfortunate situation" and said his client "did not make $1 off of this situation." Kobeissi himself, reached by LAist when the charges were first filed, said prosecutors had it "all wrong": "I should be rewarded. It's completely opposite, what they are claiming."

According to the Attorney General's office, Kobeissi's sentence includes 12 months of probation, 100 hours of community service, a National Association of Realtors ethics course, a written apology to the victims, and a $20,000 payment to a disaster-relief fund. That $20,000 is a sentencing term, not the statute's fine: Section 17500 itself caps a false-advertising fine at $2,500.

The case drew notice as, in LAist reporter David Wagner's account, one of the first criminal price-gouging charges filed after the fires — a characterization that attached to the initial charge, not to the false-advertising conviction that ultimately landed.
