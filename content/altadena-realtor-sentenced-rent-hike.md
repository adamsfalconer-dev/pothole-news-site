> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents July 2026 events.*

After the Eaton Fire, a couple who had just lost their home went looking for a place to rent. They found a listing held by Mike Kobeissi, a Southern California real estate agent — and found that the advertised rent had climbed 38 percent after the state's emergency order took effect. They walked away. On July 9, Kobeissi was sentenced over it.

The precise charge matters, and it is easy to get wrong. Kobeissi was prosecuted not by the Los Angeles County District Attorney but by the California Attorney General, Rob Bonta. And he was convicted not of price gouging but of false advertising — a misdemeanor. His attorney stressed that distinction, and it is a real one: California's price-gouging statute, which limits how much prices can rise after a declared emergency, was not the count he was found guilty of.

**Why it matters:** Emergency orders after a disaster are meant to stop exactly this — the reflex to charge more when desperate, displaced people have nowhere else to go. How the state actually enforces that promise, and under which law, tells fire survivors what protection they really have. A false-advertising conviction reaches the conduct, but not through the gouging statute written for the moment.

According to the Attorney General's office, Kobeissi's sentence includes 12 months of probation, 100 hours of community service, a National Association of Realtors ethics course, a $20,000 payment to a disaster-relief fund and a written apology to the victims.

The case drew notice as, in LAist reporter David Wagner's account, a first-of-its-kind charge when it was filed — a characterization that attached to the initial charge, not to the false-advertising conviction that ultimately landed.
