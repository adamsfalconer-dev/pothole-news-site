Pomona Unified's school board votes Wednesday on a strategic plan that would commit the district to specific numbers by 2031 — starting with how many of its students read proficiently.

"By 2031, a minimum of 60% of students will demonstrate proficiency in core literacy skills across grade levels," the plan reads. Two more targets follow: by 2031 "a minimum of 100% of students will have access to an A-G college course of study," the college-preparatory sequence, and "a minimum of 50% of students will take at least 1 college credit course."

The packet does not say what share of Pomona Unified students read proficiently now.

The plan is not meant to sit on a shelf. The packet says it is intended to "align with and inform" the district's Local Control and Accountability Plan and its annual budget — the two documents that decide where the money actually goes. A draft went to a public hearing on June 24.

It has five priorities, and the dated numbers are concentrated in two of them: academic success for all, and college and career readiness. The other three — climate and culture, facilities and operations, and quality workforce — are written as directions rather than deadlines. The climate priority, for instance, commits the district to "increase the number of positive responses on aspects of school climate, inclusion, attendance, behavior, discipline, and belonging" on its own surveys, "with measurable gains among marginalized populations."

Also Wednesday, the board takes up salary schedules for the Associated Pomona Teachers. The staff report says the schedules were "revised in accordance with the negotiated collective bargaining agreement between the District and the Associated Pomona Teachers," and that "as part of the agreement, Step 2 will be eliminated ('ghosted') effective July 1, 2026." The district says the approval is what keeps its published pay schedules accurate for state retirement reporting: the schedules "clearly identify position classifications, compensation levels, time base, and effective dates," and keeping them current is "necessary for CalSTRS reporting purposes and to ensure retirement credit and compensation are reported accurately."

Before the open session, the board meets in closed session on real-property negotiations covering more than 20 assessor parcels, on labor talks with its teacher, classified and management units, and on two litigation matters. Under the state's open-meeting law it has to identify its negotiators, the properties, and the people it is negotiating with in open session first — so 5:05 p.m. is when the public learns who is on the other side of the table.
