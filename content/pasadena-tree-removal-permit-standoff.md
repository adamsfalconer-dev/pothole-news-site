> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents a board vote of June 18, 2026, corrected from an earlier June 19 date circulated in press coverage, and enforcement action through July 10, 2026.*

The trees were supposed to be the easy part. To clean up toxic soil the Eaton Fire left behind, Pasadena Unified is digging up contaminated ground at 11 campuses — and to dig, it has to cut down trees, some more than a century old. By July 10, according to officials cited by LAist, 78 were gone.

Then the City of Pasadena told its own school district to stop. It posted stop-work notices, and — after the district kept removing trees at John Muir High School — obtained a court-authorized warrant to send a certified arborist onto campus. City spokesperson Lisa Derderian told Pasadena Now: "The city notified PUSD that they are subject to our Tree Protection Ordinance which requires a permit to remove a protected tree. PUSD continued removal activity at various sites which has resulted in the posting of stop work orders." Pasadena's tree ordinance, read directly, backs her account: it requires a permit to remove a protected tree in most zones, and its text carries no exemption for school districts or state-directed environmental cleanup. PUSD disputes that the ordinance applies at all — the district maintains, per Pasadena Now, that the state-overseen soil removal "is not subject to the City's tree ordinance," a position it continues to press with city officials.

The soil contamination behind all of it is real. The state's toxic-substances regulator, in a letter included with the district's own board report, found that contaminated soil at the 11 campuses — Blair High School, the Education Center, Field Elementary, Jefferson Children's and Teen's Center, John Muir High School, Longfellow Elementary, Octavia E. Butler Magnet, Washington Elementary STEM Magnet, San Rafael Elementary, Cleveland Elementary and Franklin Elementary — "presents an imminent and substantial threat to school property users," and that removing and replacing affected trees and vegetation is part of the required cleanup. The district's board ratified a $6,617,000 construction contract for the work in May.

On June 18 — not June 19, the date that circulated in early press coverage — the school board voted unanimously to continue the remediation while directing staff to evaluate saving some of the trees originally marked for removal. Facilities director Michael Dunning told the board the district would look at retention alternatives for as many as 57 protected trees, a figure reported by LAist and other outlets. The board's own written cost estimate for that evaluation, however, prices retention alternatives for 55 trees, at $2.42 million — a discrepancy between the number cited at the meeting and the number in the district's own budget line that neither the board report nor the press coverage explains.

Pasadena Councilmember Rick Cole, on the standoff: "This should never have escalated to this level." The district said, per the same account, that it "remains willing to work constructively with City leadership."

A community group, Friends of PUSD Trees, has organized opposition to the removals, and a 17-year-old sat for roughly eight hours in an oak marked for removal at the district's headquarters, according to ABC7. Reports have circulated that the group intends to seek a court order over the project; no filed lawsuit could be confirmed.
