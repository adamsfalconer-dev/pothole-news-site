> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents June and July 2026 events.*

The trees were supposed to be the easy part. To clean the toxic soil the Eaton Fire left behind, Pasadena Unified decided it had to dig — and to dig, it had to cut down about 200 trees, some more than a century old, across 11 school campuses. By July 10, roughly 78 of them were gone.

Then the City of Pasadena told its own school district to stop. It issued a stop-work order, posted halt notices at the sites, and obtained a warrant to send an arborist to inspect the trees — a rare instance of one public agency moving to halt another, and the sharpest turn yet in a fight that has drawn a teenage tree-sitter, a national newspaper, and a neighborhood's grief over losing old oaks.

**Why it matters:** This is not a story with an obvious villain. The soil is genuinely contaminated — testing at 13 campuses after the January 2025 fire found elevated toxic metals, primarily lead and arsenic, in ground where children play. Cleaning it means excavating one to four feet of earth, and the trees are in the way. But those trees are also decades of shade and neighborhood memory, and the city now says the district began cutting them without the permit the law requires. Both things can be true, and Pasadena is living inside the contradiction.

The school board tried to split the difference. On June 19, it voted to continue the remediation but to save as many trees as it could — up to 57 protected ones — using an "air excavation" method that clears soil from around roots without felling the tree. LAist and ColoradoBoulevard reported the vote.

It did not settle the neighborhood. A movement called Friends of PUSD Trees has fought the removals, and in one widely shared scene reported by ABC7, a 17-year-old, Paloma Muniz-Ochoa, climbed into an oak marked for the saw and stayed there about eight hours.

The city's escalation changed the legal terrain. As LAist reported, tree removal in Pasadena "requires a permit, and the school district doesn't have that," city communications staffer Lisa Derderian said — the basis for the stop-work order and the inspection warrant. Reports of a lawsuit have circulated, but no court filing has been confirmed; as of now the city's leverage is the permit and the warrant, not a case on a docket.

The money runs underneath all of it. The original full-excavation plan carried an estimate around $6.6 million, reimbursable by the state if the cleanup brings contamination down to health standards — a strong incentive to keep digging, trees or not. By July 12, the hyperlocal fight had gone national, with The Washington Post covering the standoff.
