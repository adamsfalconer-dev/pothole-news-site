> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents events of June–July 2026.*

Before Covina's Planning Commission ever voted, the block had already changed. Along East Edna Place, storefronts near Barranca Avenue closed or moved as landlords assembled the parcels for a power project — the kind of quiet, pre-approval churn that a city's official record rarely captures but neighbors feel first.

Then, on June 30, the commission recommended that the city reject the project outright.

The proposal is the Covina Reliability Project, a 110-megawatt battery energy storage system that the German energy company RWE wants to build on about 3.4 acres at 522 East Edna Place, wiring into Southern California Edison's nearby Cortez Substation. The city's own project file and the state's CEQA database lay out the specs; the city's page also records the commission's action: a recommendation to deny, reached after a hearing that drew hundreds of residents.

**Why it matters:** Battery storage is supposed to be the least objectionable piece of the energy build-out — no smokestack, no round-the-clock hum of a data center. Covina shows how contested even that has become in the San Gabriel Valley, and it raises a sharper question than the vote itself: when a site is cleared of its tenants during the entitlement fight, the community absorbs the disruption whether or not the project is ever approved.

What happens next is genuinely unsettled. A Planning Commission recommendation is advisory; the decision belongs to the City Council. The council had the item scheduled for July 8 — and then pulled it from the agenda, with no new date set. As LAist's Anjanette Gile reported, that leaves the project neither approved nor formally dead, and neighbors without an answer.

For a watchdog, the cleanest facts are the ones in the record: a named developer, a specific address, a 110-megawatt rating, a unanimous recommendation to deny, and a council vote that was placed on the calendar and then quietly removed. The displacement that came before all of it is harder to document — reported by LA Public Press, and worth the city answering for directly.
