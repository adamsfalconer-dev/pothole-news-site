Covina's planning staff recommended approving a 110-megawatt battery storage plant on Edna Place. The city's planning commission rejected that advice on June 30, voting 5-0 to recommend the council deny it over the risk of a catastrophic battery fire. Two weeks later, the council vote that will settle it has not reached an agenda.

Under AB 205, the Community Development Department wrote in its report to the commission, an applicant seeking approval of a battery storage facility "can bypass local jurisdiction approval" — a result that would allow the use inside the city "without discretionary local review or the same opportunity to leverage any type of overall community benefit as outlined in the Development Agreement." On that basis, the department recommended the commission approve the project. The commission was not persuaded.

The project would put a battery energy storage facility on about 3.4 acres at 522–680 E. Edna Place, an industrial block next to Southern California Edison's Cortez substation. The applicant, Covina Reliability, LLC, whose contact on the city's project page is listed at RWE Clean Energy, would demolish two industrial buildings totaling approximately 32,640 square feet. It has been assembling approvals since May 2025: a site plan, a conditional use permit, a variance for an 11-foot-4-inch perimeter wall, and a development agreement.

The commission's findings are dominated by public safety. The resolution describes the prospect of "a catastrophic event, including the release of harmful air pollutants" — citing thermal runaway, the difficulty of suppressing high-intensity toxic battery fires, and the risk of re-ignition — and it names the Metrolink railway, public parks and residential properties among what would be affected. It finds "adverse noise impacts," an "adverse effect on abutting property," and a failure to address five required operational standards. Separately, it finds the site "not adequate in size and shape" to fit the plant and everything the city's zoning code requires around it. Only after all of that does the resolution reach the development agreement, which it found offered the city no needed housing, no "well-recognized business or store," and no significant sales-tax or job gains.

Hundreds of residents turned out against the project, packing a June 23 hearing before the commission reconvened a week later to vote. Commissioner TJ Nass first moved to deny the project pending a full environmental impact report; the motion died for lack of a second. Commissioner Dan McMeekin then moved denial outright. The recording of the meeting ends at a recess called to draft the denial resolution, and that resolution's certification page is the record of the vote: ayes from Rosie Richardson, Nass, McMeekin, Jeanette Flores and Chair Bryan Rodriguez, with no noes, no absences and no abstentions.

The final word belongs to the city council, and that is where the record goes quiet. The vote was expected Tuesday, July 7. It never reached the posted agenda — the meeting's 760-page packet contains no battery item, and none came up at the meeting itself. Associate Planner Eduardo Lomeli told LAist the item had been pulled and that no new date had been set. The record does not say why.

Residents came anyway. Covina resident Sandra Galvin asked the council to vote against the project during public comment on July 7, LAist reported: "No parent wants to wonder what would happen if a battery storage facility experienced a fire or other emergency near their community."

The July 7 meeting seated the council that will decide. It adopted the resolution declaring the June 2 election results and administered oaths to five officials — three council members plus the city clerk and the city treasurer. Only one of the council's five seats changed hands: new District 5 member Andrew Aleman. Mayor Hector Delgado ran unopposed, and Victor Linares was re-elected to a four-year term.

The project is not dead. The city's status line reads: "Project under review w/ Los Angeles County Fire Status: Applicant to submit" — the next move is the applicant's. RWE's own project page still calls the plant "In Development," with operation targeted for 2027, and lists $17 million in property taxes over its lifespan and 150 construction jobs. The company has not publicly addressed the commission's recommendation.
