If Chino Hills sends a 1-cent sales tax to the November ballot on Tuesday, voters will spend the fall hearing round numbers — $11.5 million a year, a penny per dollar. Here is what those numbers mean, and the question the round numbers hide.

**What it is.** The measure is a *transactions-and-use tax* — a local surcharge added on top of California's base sales-tax rate. You would pay it on most taxable purchases made in the city: a $100 purchase costs an extra $1. The city estimates it raises about $11.5 million a year, roughly $144 per resident.

**Why now.** The city's 2026-27 budget spends $63.5 million against $59.2 million in revenue — a $4.3 million gap covered this year from $25.4 million in reserves. City Manager Rod Hill says those reserves run dry in three to five years if nothing changes. A sales tax is the option the council asked staff to bring back.

**The question that decides everything: general or special?** This is the fine print that matters most, and it is not yet confirmed in the public record.

- A **general tax** flows into the general fund, can be spent on anything, and passes with a **simple majority**.
- A **special tax** is earmarked for a stated purpose — roads, public safety — and needs a **two-thirds** supermajority.

General taxes are easier to pass and easier to criticize, because "for essential services" is a description, not a legal restriction. Neighboring Chino structured its own recent measure as a general tax. Which path Chino Hills chose will be visible in Tuesday's ballot-resolution language — and it tells you both how hard the measure is to pass and whether a "for public safety" pitch is a promise or a preference.

**Why it matters:** A sales tax is the rare government decision voters make directly. The difference between a general and special tax is the difference between trusting the council with the money and binding it — and that difference is decided in a resolution most residents will never read.
