> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents events of spring and summer 2026.*

On July 1, under lights at the Fairplex in Pomona, the Los Angeles Knight Riders played the first competitive cricket match at a stadium that did not exist three months earlier — and that, in 2028, is slated to host the Olympics.

The speed is the first thing to understand. Ground was broken April 22. By early July, the builder, LandTek, reported a finished ground with a roughly 200,000-square-foot outfield and seating for more than 10,000. That is a permanent-looking venue assembled in about 100 days on the grounds best known for the L.A. County Fair.

**Why it matters:** Pomona is the working heart of the eastern valley — a city more often discussed for its budget strains and its freeways than its marquees. Overnight, it is an Olympic venue and the home of a professional sports franchise. That is a genuine shift in a city's identity and its economy, the kind of thing Pomona rarely gets and should be able to examine in full. Which is the problem: the public terms of how it happened are not public.

The facility wears two names. For the 2028 Games, LA28 lists it as the "Fairgrounds Cricket Stadium," the centerpiece of the organizers' Pomona zone and the site of cricket's return to the Olympic program after 128 years, in the fast Twenty20 format, with six men's and six women's teams. The rest of the time it is the Knight Riders Cricket Ground, home of the LA Knight Riders of Major League Cricket — a club owned by Knight Riders Sports, the group co-led by the Bollywood star Shah Rukh Khan and run by chief executive Venky Mysore.

The groundbreaking drew the people who make a deal like this: Pomona Mayor Tim Sandoval, Fairplex president and chief executive Walter Marquez, Mysore, and officials from the LA28 organizing committee and the International Cricket Council. The first MLC fixtures followed July 1 through 5, opening against the defending champion Washington Freedom.

Here is what the record does not show: what the stadium cost, who paid for it, and what Pomona and Fairplex agreed to in exchange. None of the announcements — from LA28, the ICC, Fairplex or the team — puts a number on the construction or spells out the public terms. For a city hosting a franchise and an Olympic event on fairgrounds that carry public obligations, those are the questions that matter more than the final score, and they are the ones nobody has answered.

None of that dims what happened on the field, or what it signals. Cricket is one of the world's most-watched sports, and its American beachhead now runs through Pomona. The celebration is earned. The accounting is owed.
