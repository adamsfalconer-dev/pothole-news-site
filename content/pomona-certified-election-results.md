Pomona's June 2 election is certified, and this desk is reporting the outcomes for the first time — from the county registrar's own count.

The closest result: District 5, where Yvonne Cobarrubias beat Elliott Rothman 2,458 to 2,446 — a margin of 12 votes, 50.12% to 49.88%. (An earlier press account put the margin at 8 votes; the certified record says 12, and that supersedes it.) The seat was open — incumbent Steve Lustro was not on the ballot.

**Why it matters:** Twelve votes is the distance between two different councils. And Measure Z's passage — 14,250 to 6,986, a 2-to-1 ratio — permanently rewires the city's most flexible money: the "Pomona Kids First Funding Amendment" converts the Children and Youth Fund from a General Fund percentage into a guaranteed 10% of the city's Bradley-Burns sales-tax take. What that comes to in dollars won't be knowable until the adopted budget is read; we're not estimating it.

Elsewhere on the ballot, as of the certified count: Nora Garcia held District 3 outright with 71.87%, ending that race in June under the charter's majority rule. District 2 goes to a November 3 runoff — Victor Preciado (45.38%) against Jacky Elizalde (33.03%).

One documented oddity in the money: a committee named "Pomona Kids First," filing from a San Francisco address, spent $10,815.02 on literature and postage **opposing** Measure Z — the measure whose ballot nickname its own name mirrors — with cumulative spending of $21,987.73. The filing shows what was spent, not who gave; the committee's funders are unknown until its donor filings are pulled. That is a documented observation, not an accusation.

No argument against Measure Z was submitted for the ballot itself; its listed supporters included Mayor Tim Sandoval and four current or former officials.
