> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents June 2026 events.*

The peacocks were here before almost everyone arguing about them. This spring, a short documentary put the argument back on the screen.

"Our Neighbors, the Peacocks," directed by Callie Barlow and posted by the ideas site Aeon in June, revisits a divide that has run through Arcadia for generations: whether the city's free-roaming peafowl are a treasured inheritance to protect or a loud, destructive nuisance to relocate. The film does not settle it. It documents that the split is still very much alive.

The birds are a genuine piece of local history. They descend from the flocks kept by Elias "Lucky" Baldwin, the 19th-century land baron whose ranch became Arcadia, around 1879. Their spread across the city's yards and rooftops in the decades since is the reason Arcadia has spent so long legislating over what residents may and may not do about them.

The operative rule is old and lightly used. Since 1979, Arcadia has banned feeding the peafowl, with a penalty cited as up to $1,000 or six months — a law that is, in practice, essentially never prosecuted. It has proven influential anyway: when Los Angeles County pursued a countywide feeding ban in 2021, under Supervisor Kathryn Barger, the effort was modeled on Arcadia's ordinance.

What the documentary does not mark is any new policy. There is no fresh Arcadia legislation and no 2026 city action on the table; the debate the film captures is a cultural one, and the law on the books remains the 1979 ordinance. Even the basic census is unsettled — estimates of the current flock range from dozens to hundreds, and no firm count exists.

That is, in its way, the point. A city can live beside a disagreement for half a century without resolving it — feeding it just enough, despite the ordinance, to keep it going.
