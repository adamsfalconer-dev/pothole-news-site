The single largest line in Chino Hills' latest warrant run does not go to a city department. It goes to the San Bernardino County Sheriff: $1,672,104 in one month, according to the warrants attached to Tuesday's council agenda — a recurring monthly cost for policing. Annualized, that is on the order of $20 million a year — in a city now asking whether it needs a new tax to close a $4.3 million gap.

The same warrant run includes a $39,000 payment to FM3, a firm that conducts budget-priorities and ballot-measure surveys — the kind of polling a city does before it asks voters for money.

**Why it matters:** Chino Hills doesn't run its own police department; like its contract-city neighbors, it buys policing from the county. That makes the Sheriff's contract one of the city's largest ongoing obligations and among the hardest to trim, because the price is set in a county agreement, not a city budget line. When residents hear "public safety" attached to a tax measure this fall, this is the number underneath it — and it is worth seeing plainly next to the deficit it partly explains.

This is context, not accusation: contracting with the county is a normal, often cost-effective choice, and a $1.67 million monthly payment is a contract cost, not a finding. But a city weighing a sales tax owes residents the arithmetic, and the arithmetic starts here.
