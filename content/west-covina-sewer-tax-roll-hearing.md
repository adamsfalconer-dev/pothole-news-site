West Covina's City Council holds a public hearing Tuesday, July 21, on collecting the city's sewer service charge through the Los Angeles County property-tax roll for the coming year — and, for most homeowners, the takeaway is that the charge is not changing.

The sewer service charge stays at $82.02 per unit — one unit for a single home, one unit per 667 square feet of floor area for commercial property — the same rate the city has charged since the 2017-18 fiscal year. Its resolution of intention says plainly that "there is no proposed increase to the rate of the sewer service charge" for 2026-27. The July 21 hearing, held under state Health and Safety Code section 5473, is about the method of collection — whether to keep billing the charge on the county tax roll rather than separately.

Because the charge is not going up, the hearing does not come with the mailed notice a rate increase would trigger. Under Proposition 218, a city raising a fee like this must mail every affected property owner a notice of the increase and their right to protest it in writing, and must drop the increase if the owners of a majority of the affected parcels object; the city's staff report notes that mailing "is only required when the charges are increased above the current sewer service rate." Instead, notice of this hearing is published in a newspaper, and the resolution says only that the council "will hear and consider all objections or protests, if any." A resident who wants to object can still speak at the July 21 hearing or submit a written objection to the city clerk beforehand; there is no mailed notice or protest form going out this year, and no earlier deadline is set.

The one charge on the same night's agenda that does rise is the citywide Lighting and Maintenance District assessment, which pays for streetlights and related upkeep. It goes up 2 percent, from $61.20 to $62.42 per unit — an increase within a cap voters previously approved, tied to inflation, which the city says does not count as a new assessment requiring a protest ballot. Even at the higher rate, the district's assessment income of about $1.95 million falls short of its roughly $2.46 million in operating costs. Three neighborhood landscape-maintenance districts — Nos. 4, 6 and 7 — are held flat, and a business improvement district covering the city's Auto Plaza charges each of seven dealerships $16,524, unchanged and paid by the businesses, not residents.
