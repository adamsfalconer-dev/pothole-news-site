> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents events of spring and summer 2026.*

The Arroyo Seco is dry most of the year — a concrete-and-cobble wash running out of the San Gabriel Mountains. The plan to make it work harder has set three cities against one another.

Pasadena, as lead agency, and neighboring South Pasadena want to build two facilities along the arroyo to capture stormwater, clean it, recharge the groundwater beneath it, and store some for reuse — including irrigating South Pasadena's Arroyo Seco Golf Course. It is, on its face, exactly the kind of local water-supply project California keeps saying it needs.

**Why it matters:** The catch is where it would be built. One of the sites sits on parkland owned by the City of Los Angeles, at the seam where the three cities meet — and LA, which has no financial stake in the project, does not want construction on its land. The Gabrieleño Band of Mission Indians–Kizh Nation opposes it too, on sacred-site grounds. And the design would remove roughly 140 mature trees. A water project that pits drought logic against parkland, tribal heritage and a tree canopy is not a technical footnote; it is a fight about who decides what happens to a shared river.

That fight reached Los Angeles City Hall this summer. Councilmember Ysabel Jurado, whose district includes the parkland, introduced a resolution challenging the project in May. On July 1, the council did not adopt it — it referred the matter to committee, the procedural move that keeps an issue alive without taking a position. The distinction matters: LA has not formally opposed the project; it has parked the question.

The environmental review is where the specifics will finally be pinned down. Pasadena released a draft environmental impact report in May and, after pushback, extended the public-comment period to August 1. The exact numbers at the heart of the argument — how much water the project would actually capture and reuse, and whether the golf-course irrigation justifies the tree removal — vary across the coverage and are not yet nailed down in a form we'll print as fact; the EIR is where to hold the project to account. The review itself exists because a resident, Clara Solis, sued in 2024 and forced a fuller study.
