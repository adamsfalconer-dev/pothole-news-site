> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents events of spring and summer 2026, current as of mid-July.*

The Arroyo Seco is dry most of the year — a concrete-and-cobble wash running out of the San Gabriel Mountains. The plan to make it work harder has set three cities against one another.

Pasadena, as lead agency, and neighboring South Pasadena want to build two facilities along the arroyo to capture stormwater, clean it, recharge the groundwater beneath it, and store some for reuse — including irrigating South Pasadena's Arroyo Seco Golf Course.

One of the two sites sits on parkland owned by the City of Los Angeles, at the seam where the three cities meet, and Los Angeles does not want construction on its land. The Gabrieleño Band of Mission Indians–Kizh Nation opposes it too, on sacred-site grounds. The design would also remove roughly 140 mature trees.

That fight reached Los Angeles City Hall this summer. Councilmember Ysabel Jurado, whose district includes the parkland, introduced a resolution challenging the project on May 22. On July 1, the council did not adopt it — it referred the matter to the Rules, Elections and Intergovernmental Relations Committee, 14 to 0 with one member absent. As of July 14, the file has not moved: no committee hearing, no return to the full council. The distinction still matters — Los Angeles has not formally opposed the project; it has parked the question.

The environmental review is where the specifics will be pinned down. Pasadena released a draft environmental impact report in May and extended the public-comment period to August 1. The numbers at the heart of the argument — how much water the project would actually capture and reuse, and whether the golf-course irrigation justifies the tree removal — vary across the available coverage and are not yet settled in a form to print as fact.
