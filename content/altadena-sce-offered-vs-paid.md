Southern California Edison says it has now offered Eaton Fire victims nearly $730 million to settle their losses directly, without a lawsuit. By the same accounting, it has paid out nearly $300 million — roughly two-fifths of what it has offered.

Both numbers are Edison's own, from a July 9 update on its Wildfire Recovery Compensation Program, a voluntary way for people who lost homes, businesses or belongings in the January 2025 fire to settle with the company out of court; no outside auditor has checked them. The program stops taking claims after Monday, Nov. 30, 2026.

Some of the gap is timing. An offer has to be made, accepted and signed before any money moves — Edison says it pays within 30 days once a settlement's conditions are met — and the company says many more payments are in process. In a June update it reported that more than 73 percent of its offers had been accepted and fewer than 1 percent turned down.

By its July 9 count, Edison had extended about 2,100 offers, reaching more than 5,100 claimants, and had paid nearly 2,000 of those claimants. More than 3,800 claims have been submitted, the company said, covering nearly 11,700 people, trusts and legal entities; 31 percent were filed by attorneys or other representatives.

The choice at the center of the program is a legal one, and Edison states it plainly. Filing a claim "does not waive a claimant's rights, including the right to pursue litigation in the future," the company says, and neither does receiving or weighing an offer. What gives up those rights is accepting one: a claimant keeps "all legal rights until accepting a settlement offer and signing a full release, which waives all past and future claims related to the Eaton Fire" against Edison and companies tied to it. The release has to be signed and notarized. A resident can file, read an offer, and still sue; signing is the point of no return.

Edison runs the program without conceding it is to blame. Its own terms say taking part "does not constitute an admission of liability or wrongdoing" and that the company "expressly denies responsibility for the underlying events." The company has said more than that disclaimer does, though: in a February statement to investors, it called it "likely" that its equipment "could have been associated with the ignition of the Eaton Fire." The fire's cause has not been officially determined, and the utility faces lawsuits over it.

Not everyone considers the offers fair. The Eaton Fire Survivors Network, an advocacy group, has called them too low. Its executive director, Joy Chen, a former Los Angeles deputy mayor, has said that for a solvent, profitable Edison "to offer less than a bankrupt company did is indefensible." Amanda Riddle, a lawyer suing Edison on behalf of fire claimants, has warned that survivors facing a long wait for a trial could feel pressure to accept "at a deep discount," and has pressed the company toward "a full, fair and negotiated settlement process" — comments both reported by Pasadena Now. Edison says the program exists to give people a faster option than a court fight, and that it has been delivering offers within 35 days of a completed claim, well inside the 90 days it committed to.

For a household weighing the choice, the terms are broad and the arithmetic is Edison's. The program covers owners and tenants alike, for total and partial loss of homes, commercial property and other buildings, business interruption, smoke, soot and ash damage, physical injury and death. A fully documented claim draws an offer within 90 days, the company says, or within about nine months on a detailed-review track for more complex claims. Claims are filed through the program's website. Whatever the figure comes back as, it is the claimant's to take or refuse — and refusing keeps the courthouse open.
