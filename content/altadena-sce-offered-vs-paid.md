Eighteen months after the Eaton Fire, Southern California Edison said in a July 9 update that its compensation program has extended 2,100 offers — a single offer can cover multiple claimants — to more than 5,100 claimants, totaling nearly $730 million. The same update puts the money actually paid at nearly $300 million, to just under 2,000 claimants.

The money flows from Edison because the fire may have started with Edison. The company has said throughout the investigation that, absent additional evidence, it believes it is "likely that its equipment could have been associated with the ignition" of the January 2025 fire, which killed 19 people and destroyed more than 9,400 structures in and around Altadena; plaintiffs' lawyers have gone further, asking a judge to rule — without a jury — that SCE ignited it, a motion the company calls "wrong on the facts and the law." The official cause remains undetermined, per CAL FIRE. Announcing the compensation program last July, Edison International's chief executive said community members "shouldn't have to wait for the final conclusions in the Eaton Fire investigation," casting direct payment as the alternative to "lengthy, expensive litigation." The program accepts claims from people already suing the company, filed through their lawyers.

Both sets of numbers come from SCE itself, and the company attributes the distance between them to process: an offer becomes a check only after a claimant accepts it and the settlement agreement's conditions are met — payment then follows within 30 days, it says, often faster. What its releases don't break down is the unpaid balance: how much sits with claimants still weighing their offers versus accepted offers awaiting checks. What they do show is that roughly three of every five claimants holding an offer had not been paid as of July 9.

The program's scale, according to SCE: more than 3,800 claims covering nearly 11,700 people, trusts and legal entities, with just under a third of the claims filed through attorneys or other authorized representatives. In June the company said more than 70% of offers extended to that point had been accepted, and that offers go out within 35 days of a substantially complete claim on average — inside its 90-day commitment.

What the money has looked like in practice, in SCE's June examples of actual compensation: for the average homeowner who lost a house, a total of $1,700,627 — $754,204 paid through the program, plus $946,423 in average insurance coverage. The company's largest homeowner example totaled about $6.1 million, most of it insurance. Renters with smoke and ash damage averaged $44,368 through the program; the lowest payment it lists for that kind of damage is $15,000.

Whose word is all this? SCE's. The program was designed in consultation with Kenneth Feinberg and Camille Biros, architects of the Sept. 11 victim compensation fund — but by design they are not involved in reviewing claims or running the program. The company also engaged RAND to independently assess how properties are valued for offers; that review, as SCE describes it, does not extend to the running totals the company reports. The payment figures are the utility's own accounting.

The practical terms, as SCE lays them out: filing is free — no application, administrative or legal fees — takes under two hours on average, and waives no rights, including the right to sue later. Accepting an offer is what settles a claim. Eligibility runs broader than owners of destroyed homes: renters, businesses, people with smoke and ash damage, physical injuries or lost family members, and claimants already in litigation can all file under the program's terms. Claims involving children require court approval of the settlement. Filing runs through Nov. 30 at sce.com/directclaims.
