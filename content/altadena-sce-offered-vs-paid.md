> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents June 2026 events.*

Southern California Edison has a headline number for its Eaton Fire recovery program: more than $650 million. It is worth reading that number carefully, because it is money offered — not money paid.

The utility's Wildfire Recovery Compensation Program is a voluntary way for Eaton Fire victims to settle directly with the company, out of court, before a filing deadline of Nov. 30, 2026. In a June 4 update, SCE laid out where the program stood — and the figures tell two different stories depending on which line you read.

On the offer side: SCE said it had made about 1,900 offers to more than 4,600 claimants, totaling more than $650 million. More than 3,500 claims had been filed, representing roughly 10,700 individuals, trusts and entities.

On the paid side, the number is smaller. Only about 1,500 claimants had actually been paid, the company said, totaling more than $200 million.

**Why it matters:** The distance between those figures is the story. "More than $650 million" and "10,000-plus" have circulated as shorthand for how much Edison is doing for fire survivors. But offered is not paid, and filed is not compensated: by SCE's own accounting, roughly 10,700 people are represented in filed claims while about 1,500 claimants have money in hand. For a household that lost everything in January 2025, that gap is the difference between a press release and a rebuilt home.

The averages hint at what an offer looks like. SCE put the average total-loss offer to a homeowner at $754,204 and the average smoke-and-ash offer to a tenant at $44,368 — a reminder that "an offer" spans a vast range depending on what was lost.

Two caveats matter here. First, the company makes no admission that it caused the Eaton Fire; it frames the program as relief, not liability. Second, filing a claim does not sign away a victim's right to sue — only accepting an offer does. That is the trade at the center of every one of these decisions.

And one more, which is the reason to treat all of the above as a starting point rather than a scorecard: every figure here comes from Southern California Edison's own press releases. No independent auditor has verified the offers, the payments or the counts. The company is both the party writing the checks and the party reporting how many it has written.
