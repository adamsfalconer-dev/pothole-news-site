The First Public Hydrogen Authority — the hydrogen-utility venture City of Industry co-owns with the City of Lancaster — is carrying a net position of negative $5.2 million, according to its own unaudited treasurer's report for the period ended May 31. Its entire startup has been financed by one lender: Lancaster, whose $6.6 million promissory note is now $5.6 million drawn, with $1 million left.

**Why it matters:** A city of roughly 113 voters co-governs a joint powers authority betting millions of public dollars on a hydrogen market that doesn't yet pay its bills — and nobody else is reading these packets. Industry's man on the board is Councilmember Newell Ruggles, the authority's vice chair, who draws a $1,600-a-month stipend for the seat. That fact corrects our own desk file, which wrongly said Industry doesn't sit on the FPH2 board.

The numbers, from the June 18 board packet: $3.4 million spent this past fiscal year against $268,986 in revenue — $250,000 of it "consulting services income." Legal services ran 70% over budget: $440,921 against $260,000 budgeted, spread across four firms. Board stipends total $112,000 for the year to date, one of them invoiced by an LLC, "Vasquez Impact Solutions LLC."

The turnaround plan was on the same June 18 agenda: a budget for the fiscal year that began July 1 projecting $8.15 million in hydrogen-sales revenue against $5.85 million in purchases — the authority's first year of actual product sales, with staff reporting offtake agreements "being finalized" for delivery "early in the next fiscal year." Whether the board adopted that budget is not yet public: no minutes of the June 18 meeting have posted anywhere. The board also went into closed session that day to negotiate over a parcel, APN 3378-001-001, with parties named Wen and Mang Huang.

To be clear: no wrongdoing is alleged here. This is a map of where public money sits — a startup deficit, a single-lender lifeline, and a revenue plan that hasn't been confirmed as adopted.
