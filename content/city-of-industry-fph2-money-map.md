The public hydrogen authority that City of Industry co-founded closed its first full fiscal year $5.8 million in debt to the City of Lancaster, with liabilities outrunning assets by $5.4 million — and on Thursday its board will try again to adopt the budget that is supposed to turn that around.

The First Public Hydrogen Authority, the joint powers agency Industry and Lancaster formed in December 2024 to buy and sell hydrogen, has since grown: its website lists Fresno, Montebello, Shafter and the Rowland Water District as member agencies alongside the founding cities. The board was supposed to adopt its 2026-27 budget on June 18. Draft minutes up for approval Thursday show the board declined: after Chief Executive Officer Jason Caudle presented what the minutes describe as a balanced budget "reflecting contracts that are nearing execution and contracts currently executed," the board decided more review was needed and voted unanimously to put adoption off to its July 16 meeting, passing a stopgap resolution to keep paying bills in the meantime.

The budget coming back Thursday is a different document. Projected hydrogen sales — the authority's first — are cut about 16%, from $8.15 million to $6.87 million, a change the staff report attributes to "further refinement on when sales activity is projected to begin." Projected consulting revenue nearly triples, from $1 million to $2.75 million, "to reflect additional services that are being provided." The projected surplus grows from $238,697 to $1.46 million. The hydrogen revenue still rests on deals in progress: staff "continues to work diligently to finalize agreements with potential offtakers," with delivery expected "early in the next fiscal year." No buyer is named anywhere in the packet.

The books that budget is meant to fix are in the treasurer's unaudited year-end report, on the same agenda. The authority spent $3.68 million in 2025-26 — 87% of its budget — and earned $270,530, of which $250,000 was consulting income. Legal services ran 78% over budget: $463,478 against $260,000. The new budget raises the legal line to $360,000. Assets of $561,491 against just under $6 million in liabilities put the authority's net position at negative $5.4 million.

Nearly all of that liability is one credit line. Lancaster approved a $2.6 million promissory note in January 2025 and expanded it to $6.6 million that August; $5.6 million is drawn, and with $218,292 in accrued interest at 3.816%, the authority owed Lancaster $5,818,292 as of June 30. What remains is $355,541 in cash — about five weeks of spending at last year's average pace — plus $1 million of borrowing room on the note. The staff report says it "may be necessary to draw down a portion of the remaining available funds to cover cash flow needs during the coming fiscal year," balanced budget or not.

Industry's seat at the table is Councilmember Newell Ruggles, on the council since 2015 and the authority's vice chair; Lancaster Mayor R. Rex Parris chairs the board. Board members are paid $1,600 a month, and stipends totaled $123,200 last year. June's payment run lists seven $1,600 stipends: five to board members directly, one to the City of Lancaster, and one, listed as a board stipend, paid to Vasquez Impact Solutions LLC — the packet does not say which member's stipend the city or the company payment covers.

Thursday's agenda also sends the board into closed session to evaluate Caudle's job performance.
