> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents June 2026 events.*

A student in the Walnut Valley Unified School District can score at the very top of California's English-proficiency test and still be labeled an "English learner" the next fall. The reason isn't the state exam — it's the extra bar the district sets before it will call a student fluent.

That gap is the subject of a June 20 analysis by EdSource reporter Zaidee Stavely, which found California districts use inconsistent standards to reclassify English learners — keeping many students in the category longer than the state's own test would. Here's how it works: students who enter California schools speaking another language are designated English learners and take the ELPAC, the state's English-proficiency exam, each year. A 4 is the top score. But earning a 4 does not, by itself, reclassify a student as "fluent English proficient." Districts layer on their own requirements, most commonly a benchmark on the Smarter Balanced test, the state's academic exam in English language arts.

**Why it matters:** The label is not just paperwork. A student who tests proficient in English can remain an "English learner" on the district's books for years because of a separate academic bar — and the longer it lingers, the more it can shape the classes that student is placed in. EdSource found the delays are common and, in some cases, extreme.

The picture around Pomona is remarkably uniform, with one exception. EdSource compared Claremont, Walnut Valley, Chino Valley and Bonita, and found all four require students to meet or exceed standards on the Smarter Balanced test or a similar reading assessment. Only Pomona Unified goes further, adding a separate writing exam. For Walnut Valley specifically, the analysis identified that reading benchmark — but it did not lay out the district's complete reclassification criteria, and neither do we. The rest of what a Walnut Valley student must clear should be confirmed with the district, in its board-adopted reclassification policy.

The statewide stakes are stark. Among students reclassified in 2024-25, EdSource reported that about a quarter waited more than nine months after scoring a 4 on the ELPAC, and more than one in ten waited two years or longer. The single longest documented delay was 1,222 days — more than three years spent classified as an English learner after testing at the top of the state's proficiency exam.

A bill in the Legislature would collapse that extra step. AB 2555, from Assemblymember Darshana Patel, would make an ELPAC score of 4 sufficient on its own to reclassify a student — stripping districts of the power to require a separate academic benchmark on top of it.
