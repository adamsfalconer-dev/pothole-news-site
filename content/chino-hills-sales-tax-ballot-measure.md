Shoppers in Chino Hills pay 7.75% in sales tax today. If voters approve the measure their council placed on the November ballot Tuesday, they would pay 8.75% — and there is no date on which it stops.

The council voted unanimously on Tuesday, July 14 to put the Chino Hills Services Measure before voters on Nov. 3, the city said in an announcement two days later. The tax would add one cent to every taxable dollar spent inside the city and raise about $11.5 million a year, by the city's estimate.

Approval would not change receipts right away. Under the ordinance, collection could begin only with a calendar quarter starting more than 110 days after the council formally declares the result — which makes April 2027 the earliest possible start.

That money would go into the general fund. The draft ordinance is explicit about it: the proceeds "shall be for unrestricted general revenue purposes of the city," with no requirement that any of it be spent on any particular thing. That is also why the measure passes on a simple majority — the resolution placing it on the ballot sets the bar at "a majority (50 +1) of the votes cast." Under state law, a tax raised for a specific dedicated purpose needs two-thirds. The structure is one state law provides for city sales taxes, and it is common: the state tax department's list of local add-on taxes runs to more than 300 cities, and Diamond Bar's council votes Tuesday on placing the same kind of measure — a general tax, decided by simple majority — on the same November ballot.

The ordinance does require an accounting. Section 3.30.180 puts the penny's revenue under the same independent annual audit as the rest of the general fund, and requires the auditor's report to carry "an accounting of the revenues received and expenditures made from the transactions and use tax," presented each year to the council and "made available for public review." What the ordinance does not do is create an oversight committee.

The measure does not expire. The ordinance says the tax "shall remain in effect until repealed by the voters," and the question voters will see puts it more plainly: the penny runs "until ended by voters."

## What the council says it is for

The city has budgeted to spend more than it expects to take in: $63.5 million in spending against $59.2 million in revenue for the fiscal year that began this month — a $4.3 million gap, and the second straight year of one. The city projects that gap grows to about $8.4 million by 2030-31 and drains its reserves.

The penny would raise more than either figure. At roughly $11.5 million a year, it is more than two and a half times this year's budgeted gap, and more than the one the city projects five years out.

The council's own resolution points at policing. "Public safety costs have increased by $2.4 million since last year," it says, and the same resolution puts public safety at about 34% of what the city spends from its general fund. Chino Hills does not run its own police department; it contracts with the San Bernardino County Sheriff's Department.

The ballot question lists what the city says the penny would protect: response times, wildfire preparation, crime prevention, clean public areas, water resources, open space — "with audits, spending disclosure, all funds used locally." The audit is written into the ordinance. The list of services is not. None of it binds the council, which is what an unrestricted general tax means.

## How to be heard before November

At most one argument for the measure and one against will be printed in the ballot pamphlet every Chino Hills voter receives. Whoever writes them has to file by the middle of next month.

The council can write the argument in favor itself, and state law ranks its argument first if it does. It is not the only one who may file. Under the rules the council adopted July 14, an argument may come from the council, from council members it authorizes, from any voter eligible to vote on the measure, from a bona fide association of citizens, or from some combination of those. If more than one arrives on the same side, the city clerk selects according to an order of preference state law sets.

An argument runs no more than 300 words and must be received in the City Clerk's office — on the second floor of City Hall, 14000 City Center Drive — by 11:30 a.m. on Monday, Aug. 17. It needs the printed name and signature of each author, no more than five of which will be printed, and it must come with a signed statement on a form the City Clerk provides — the form appears at page 213 of the July 14 agenda packet. The city lists the clerk's office at 909-364-2620 and cityclerk@chinohills.org. The rules the council adopted do not say whether an argument may be mailed or emailed. They say only that it must be received.

Rebuttals are capped at 250 words and due by 4:30 p.m. on Friday, Aug. 21, but the rebuttal round is not a second chance to get into the pamphlet. Under the elections-code section the council adopted for this measure, a rebuttal may come only from the authors of a selected argument — each side answering the other — or from someone they authorize in writing.
