> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents financing approved in January 2026 and announced as closed in June 2026.*

The old Ontario Airport Hotel, sold off in a foreclosure sale two years ago, is being rebuilt into a Hyatt Regency, financed through a tax-exempt bond program the city and a state financing authority each capped at $100 million.

On January 20, the Ontario City Council voted 4-0-1 to let the California Statewide Communities Development Authority issue tax-exempt revenue bonds, capped at $100 million, for the redevelopment at 700 N. Haven Avenue. The council also found, in a separate vote the same night, that the project — to be built by Core Hotel Venture, LLC, an affiliate of the nonprofit developer National Community Renaissance of California, known as National CORE — serves the public interest. Council Member Daisy Macias recused herself from both votes, citing her employment.

National CORE bought the hotel in a foreclosure sale that closed in August 2024. Press accounts of the deal describe a 295-room hotel, down from about 309; the city's environmental clearance for the same project describes a narrower scope: redevelopment "limited to internal improvements," and it states that "the number of rooms will stay the same." Reopening under the Hyatt Regency flag is targeted for early 2027, according to National CORE's release.

The overall price tag is consistent, within a few percent, across every account of the deal. National CORE's June release puts the total at approximately $103 million — about $74.5 million in tax-exempt bonds plus about $27.3 million in PACE, a property-assessed clean energy financing program, figures that by the release's own math add to $101.8 million. Two outlets that covered the financing separately put it a little higher: one, citing the deal's adviser, JLL, at $77.1 million in bonds plus $26.5 million in PACE — about $103.6 million; the other, citing the PACE administrator and bond underwriter directly, at $77 million and $26 million — $103 million. All three land within about 2 percent of the same total; they differ more on how much of it counts as bonds versus the separate PACE assessment.

The city's approval in January capped only the bond portion at $100 million. The bond authority's own board, in a March planning document, capped the combined bond-and-PACE package at that same $100 million figure — a different scope than what the city voted on. Neither record states what the financing actually totaled once it closed in June.
