> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents a July 2026 ruling in a case filed that spring.*

South El Monte's mayor is suing the city she leads — and on July 7, a federal judge decided the heart of her case can proceed.

Mayor Gloria Olmos filed her lawsuit in March in U.S. District Court, a civil-rights claim under Section 1983 alleging the city retaliated against her. The city asked the court to throw it out. In an order dated July 7, Judge Percy Anderson did so only in part: he dismissed the three council members Olmos had named — Larry Rodriguez, Hector Delgado and Rudy Bojorquez — on legislative immunity, and dismissed two counts seeking declaratory and injunctive relief. But he otherwise denied the city's motion, and gave South El Monte until July 28 to answer.

**Why it matters:** A mayor taking her own city to federal court is the kind of intramural fight that usually ends quietly in a closed session. This one didn't — and a judge has now found that at least part of Olmos's claim is serious enough to move forward. For a small city, that means a discovery fight, legal bills, and a public airing of how its council treated its own mayor.

It is worth being precise about what is and isn't established. Olmos is the plaintiff; no one here is accused of a crime, and the case is civil. Her account — that a censure and related sanctions were payback, tied to her opposition to commercial cannabis in the city — comes from her own filings, and the granular details of the censure rest on secondary sources rather than an independent record. What the court record shows cleanly is narrower and more consequential: the suit exists, the city's bid to end it mostly failed, and the city must now respond.
