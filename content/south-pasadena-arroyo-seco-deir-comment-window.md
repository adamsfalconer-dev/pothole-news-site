The state agency that regulates water quality in the Los Angeles River watershed has told Pasadena that its draft environmental study for the Arroyo Seco Water Reuse Project is wrong in several places, and that a permit the project needs could be refused.

In a July 2 comment letter running to seven numbered points, the Los Angeles Regional Water Quality Control Board called findings in the study "inaccurate," wrote that it "erroneously identifies San Rafael Creek as a storm drain" when the creek "is not a stormwater facility but a water of the state," and warned that "issuance of a CWA Section 401 Water Quality Certification may be denied should the project not meet this criterion." The board asked for compensatory mitigation and for an analysis of the cities' water rights that the study does not contain.

The board also disputes the project's stated legal driver. "Several sections in the DEIR specifically state that the Project is required to meet the TMDL or that the Los Angeles Water Board has mandated the Project's implementation," the board wrote of the pollutant limit it administers. Its conclusion: "the Project is not required by the TMDL or the MS4 Permit, it will not help comply with the TMDL in wet weather, and it will only help comply with the TMDL in dry weather below the diversion point in San Rafael Creek." Approving a watershed plan, it added, "does not constitute approval of specific projects in the WMP or a mandate that those specific projects be implemented."

The two documents describe the obligation in different words. The study's stated objectives include capturing dry-weather flows to treat bacteria "as required in the bacteria Total Maximum Daily Load," and a line in its funding section — explaining why Pasadena sought state grants — calls fixing a single outfall "an unfunded State mandate for Pasadena." The bacteria limit's own codified text sets pollutant-reduction targets and compliance deadlines for the agencies that hold stormwater permits.

The board is not asking Pasadena to drop the work. It closed by saying it "welcome[s] the opportunity to work with you and the project proponent to make this project an example of environmental sustainability in California."

All of it sits in a document the public can comment on until 11:59 p.m. Saturday, August 1. Because that is a receipt deadline falling on a night when Pasadena's public-works counter is closed, email — to cmonde@cityofpasadena.net — is the channel that reliably arrives in time; the full instructions are at the end of this story.

The project would build two stormwater facilities along the Arroyo Seco — a 1.4-acre site at San Rafael in Pasadena and a 2.2-acre site at San Pascual spanning South Pasadena and Los Angeles. They would capture runoff, clean it, and put most of it back into the ground.

## The study's water figures do not add up, and its own source memo shows why

The study leads with an average annual water supply benefit of 320 acre-feet: about 258 acre-feet of groundwater recharge, and about 30 acre-feet — "less than 10 percent of captured water," it says — piped off to irrigate the Arroyo Seco Golf Course. The same figures appear in the study's summary, its introduction and its water chapter. They do not add up. The two named components total 288, leaving 32 acre-feet unaccounted for against the 320 the study leads with.

The study's performance table for the San Pascual site is sourced to a September 2022 memo by Craftwater Engineering, published in the study's second volume. The memo's version of the same table is identical to the study's in every column but one: where the study prints 30 acre-feet of irrigation reuse, the memo prints 62. The memo's numbers reconcile — 258 and 62 make 320. The study's do not. And the memo carries a second table the study does not reproduce, which breaks the 62 into its parts: 30 acre-feet for the Arroyo Seco Golf Course, and 32 for Arroyo Park.

The gap and the park's share are the same size. Whether that is the explanation, the study does not say. What it says is that "the annual irrigation water demand has been estimated at 30 af/year for the Arroyo Seco Golf Course" — matching the memo's golf-course line exactly — and its summary table appears to take its irrigation figure from one table of its source and its total from another. Why the total stayed at 320 rather than falling to 288 is not addressed. A fifth passage, in the chapter the study reserves for other required considerations, describes the 320 differently again — as capture "through infiltration," with the 30 acre-feet additional rather than included.

Nothing in either volume explains the difference. The number was in circulation before the study was published: a community group's June 2025 scoping letter refers to "the 62 acre-feet guaranteed to the City of South Pasadena for golf course irrigation as stated in the Community Outreach Meeting on April 27, 2023."

## The trees

The study counts 201 trees in the survey area as of October 2025, and proposes that 136 of them be removed or encroached on — six at San Rafael, 119 on the South Pasadena portion of San Pascual, and 11 on the Los Angeles portion. Twenty-nine are protected under the three cities' ordinances and 14 more are classed as significant.

The 136 is a ceiling, not a felling schedule, and the study says so: "Tree encroachment is assumed to result in a tree loss and is therefore considered as an impact for purposes of this EIR. However, in reality, encroachment may or may not result in a tree loss or other negative outcomes." Its table carries the note "Removals include encroachments." The study commits to replacing 50 trees, the number the ordinances require, and says its landscape plan would plant 216 in total.

Eleven of the project's 45 coast live oaks would come out, and the study names them one by one. Its tables give each removal a number and a trunk diameter — nine on the South Pasadena side of San Pascual, two on the Los Angeles side, and none at San Rafael, where all 22 oaks would stay. Oaks are protected by ordinance in all three cities. The Department of Fish and Wildlife, reviewing the same document, asked Pasadena to rewrite its tree-mitigation measure so that "all oak trees shall be replaced at a 10:1 mitigation ratio" and monitored for a decade — language the study does not currently contain. Eighty-two of the 201 are Mexican fan palms, which the study calls a fire hazard and whose removal is one of the project's stated objectives.

Fish and Wildlife also put the removal at "approximately 108 native and nonnative trees," but attributed that figure to the study itself — "According to the EIR" — and the study's own tables do not produce it.

## An alternative that kept most of the mature trees was studied and set aside

The study's alternatives chapter examined a San Pascual Park Tree Conservation Alternative, which it found would meet the project's golf-irrigation objective while conserving "a large majority of the mature trees." It uses that phrase four times — once in its executive summary and three times in the alternatives chapter. In one place it puts the claim differently, and the difference matters: the alternative would conserve "a majority of the mature trees—albeit the majority being invasive Mexican Fan Palm species."

Pasadena set the alternative aside, saying it would increase impacts on biological resources by cutting the inflow that feeds a man-made wetland, produce "less attractive" aesthetics, cost the site an existing baseball field, require broader construction with greater air-quality, greenhouse-gas and noise impacts, and raise wildfire risk "due to the non-removal of the invasive 82 Mexican Fan Palms and lack of vegetation management." It meets seven of the project's 12 objectives, the study says; removing the palms, which the study calls a fire hazard, is one of the five it does not meet.

## What the Kizh Nation asked for

Pasadena's study reports two tribal consultations, and they ended differently.

The first, during a 2023 review of an earlier version of the project, closed by "mutual agreement," the study says, after the Gabrieleño Band of Mission Indians–Kizh Nation "confirmed their satisfaction" with three mitigation measures making the Band the tribal monitor during ground disturbance. The study is specific that this happened "during the MND process" — the earlier review, which produced a mitigated negative declaration rather than the full environmental impact report now out for comment.

The second consultation is the one for this study. Pasadena notified the Band in April 2025; the Band requested consultation. That consultation, the study says, "came to a close once it was understood that the Project remains unchanged from what was formerly proposed and previously discussed with Kizh Nation during the prior consultation, and that the same mitigation measures agreed upon by Pasadena and Kizh Nation would still be implemented for the Project." That is Pasadena's account of why it treated the matter as settled: the old agreement still stood. The study records no fresh agreement and no fresh statement of satisfaction.

Two and a half months later the Band put its position in writing, in a letter published in the study's own appendices. "We are formally requesting full avoidance of this location," wrote Hereditary Chief Andrew Salas on June 30, 2025, asking that the project "be reassessed with full tribal consultation during the environmental review." On the earlier consultation, Salas wrote that it covered "a smaller component of the project" and that "there was never a complete formal consultation conducted with our tribe under CEQA or AB 52, as required by law." A second member of the Band, Ricardo Montijo, wrote the same day registering "a formal opposition to the Arroyo Seco Water Reuse Project as currently proposed." Both letters are comments on the 2025 notice that preceded this study, not on the study itself.

Pasadena's answer is in the study, and it points at a document. The letter it sent the Band in 2023 to open the earlier consultation "described and illustrated two separate sites that are identical to the scope and location of the Project as currently proposed." That letter is published in the same volume, and it does describe the same two sites at the same acreages, in a sentence repeated word for word in the 2025 letter. The 2023 letter carries a different project name: the Arroyo Seco Water Reuse and Natural Stream Restoration Project.

The study finds the project's impacts on tribal cultural resources less than significant with the three monitoring measures in place. The Band asked the city to avoid the site.

## At Los Angeles City Hall

A resolution stating that "the City of Los Angeles hereby OPPOSES the Arroyo Seco Water Reuse Project" has been introduced but not adopted. Councilmember Ysabel Jurado introduced it May 22. On July 1 the council voted 14-0, with one member absent, to refer both it and a replacement version to its Rules, Elections and Intergovernmental Relations Committee. It was still pending in committee on July 15. Los Angeles has taken no position; a councilmember has asked it to take one.

## How to comment, and by when

Comments must reach Christina Monde, P.E., City of Pasadena Department of Public Works, 100 North Garfield Avenue, Suite N306, Pasadena, CA 91101, or cmonde@cityofpasadena.net, by 11:59 p.m. on Saturday, August 1. The deadline is a receipt deadline, not a postmark deadline, and it falls late on a Saturday night at a public-works counter that will not be open — which makes email the only channel that reliably arrives in time.

Two dates are in circulation, and the safe one is July 31. The state's CEQAnet listing for the study shows a state review period ending July 31 and a public review period ending August 1 on the same page. Pasadena's own extension notice, which is the instrument that sets the deadline, says August 1 at 11:59 p.m. A comment filed by July 31 satisfies both. The deadline has already moved twice: the original 45-day period closed June 17, a first extension carried it to July 2, and Pasadena's second extension added 30 more days.
