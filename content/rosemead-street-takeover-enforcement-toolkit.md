> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents April 2026 events.*

California has spent five years building an escalating legal arsenal against street takeovers — license suspensions, 30-day vehicle seizures, a statewide definition of the crime itself. And yet one of the newer tools the state authorized has never reached the San Gabriel Valley at all.

That gap is worth understanding right now because of what happened at 1:47 a.m. on April 12, when gunfire tore through an active street takeover at Del Mar and Garvey avenues in Rosemead. Four people were wounded — a 17-year-old and three men between 19 and 22 — all of them expected to survive. No one has been arrested, and the shooter or shooters remain unidentified.

The shooting is the reason takeovers are back in the local conversation. The more useful question is what, exactly, California has armed its cities with to stop them — and where the San Gabriel Valley still comes up short. Here is the toolkit, built law by law.

**The core ban.** California Vehicle Code section 23109 has long outlawed speed contests. The recent additions target the sideshow as an event, not just the driving.

**License suspensions (AB 3, 2021).** This law lets courts suspend the driver's license of someone who participates in a sideshow. It became operative July 1, 2025 — the enforcement clock is new.

**Closing the parking-lot loophole (AB 2000, 2022).** Organizers had migrated to private, off-street lots; this extended the prohibition to cover them.

**The September 2024 package.** In a single signing, the governor added three tools: vehicle impound for sideshow involvement (AB 2186); one statewide legal definition of "sideshow" and "street takeover," so agencies enforce the same thing (AB 2807); and, most aggressively, AB 3085, which lets authorities seize a vehicle used as an "instrumentality" of a takeover for up to 30 days — without arresting anyone.

**Why it matters:** Read together, these laws move enforcement away from having to catch a driver mid-donut and toward hitting the car, the license and the crowd. That matters because the crowd is the event; a takeover without spectators is just a few cars in an intersection.

**The gap.** One tool the state authorized has not reached here. The AB 645 speed-camera pilot, passed in 2023, allows automated speed enforcement — but only in six named cities: Los Angeles, San José, Oakland, Glendale, Long Beach and San Francisco. Not one is in the San Gabriel Valley. Rosemead and its neighbors sit outside the experiment, whatever they make of it.

**The local model, and its legal ceiling.** The most aggressive nearby approach is Pico Rivera's. Its ordinance makes merely spectating within 500 feet of a takeover a $2,000 administrative citation and lets the city pursue vehicles as a nuisance for forfeiture. But there is now a constitutional ceiling over that strategy: in September 2025, the Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals, in Garcia v. County of Alameda, found a near-identical spectator ordinance unconstitutional on First Amendment grounds. The ruling has pushed agencies back toward the state Vehicle and Penal Code sections, which rest on conduct rather than mere presence.

Does any of it work? The Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department, which polices Rosemead through its Temple Station under contract, reports that takeovers across the unincorporated areas and contract cities it patrols fell 33% from 2024 to 2025 and 53% from 2024 to 2026, and it runs a joint Street Takeover Task Force with the CHP. Those are the department's own figures, and they describe a regional trend, not a Rosemead-specific count.
