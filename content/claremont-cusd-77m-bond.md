Claremont Unified is asking voters for $77 million in November to repair the roofs, heating and plumbing of its aging schools — and it says the measure would not raise the tax rate residents already pay.

The bond would levy about $32 per $100,000 of assessed value — roughly $160 a year on a home assessed at $500,000, and about $5 million a year across the district while the bonds are outstanding. It would do so, in the ballot measure's own words, "without increasing current taxes." The claim rests on timing. That rate is close to what Claremont property owners already pay on Measure Y, a $48.9 million school bond voters passed in 2000, which the district expects to finish paying off around 2028. The new bond would begin in 2029, the district says, so the rate would hold steady instead of dropping. "You never feel a decrease, you never feel an increase, you just keep paying what you've been paying over the last eight years," Superintendent Jim Elsasser told the Claremont Courier this spring.

The board took up the measure in June, and the Claremont Courier, which covered the meeting, reported that it voted to send the $77 million bond to the ballot; the district has not yet posted the signed resolution or the minutes. The measure is not formally on the ballot yet either — that turns on the district meeting a county filing deadline in early August.

To pass, the measure needs 55 percent of the vote — the lower bar California set for school bonds under Proposition 39, which took effect in late 2000. Measure Y, approved months before that law, needed a two-thirds majority; it drew 68.85 percent.

The measure authorizes a broad list of work — repairing or replacing roofs, heating and air conditioning, plumbing and electrical systems, improving safety and security, upgrading restrooms, and "physical education facilities (including turf and fields)." It names no specific campus or project; that general language is standard for a Proposition 39 bond. District officials have described the need in more concrete terms, pointing to roofs and pipes they say are worn out. "We're looking at end of life, like the roofing — that's not a routine maintenance — or plumbing," said Desiree Reyes, the district's assistant superintendent of business services. Elsasser has put the cost of the district's most urgent repairs at about $81 million in today's dollars, or roughly $102 million once several years of construction-cost increases are figured in.

Like other bonds of its kind, the measure comes with strings written into it: an independent citizens' committee to watch the spending, annual financial and performance audits, a bar on what the measure calls inappropriate administrator salaries, and a requirement that the funds stay in Claremont.

None of it could be spent on the district's day-to-day budget. Bond money is restricted by law to buildings and equipment, and Claremont Unified is separately carrying an operating shortfall: it projects spending about $1.6 million more than it takes in for 2026-27, a gap it narrowed from nearly $11 million — a roughly $9.2 million improvement it credits to a mix of departmental cuts and a state cost-of-living increase it has called unusually large. Passing the bond would not close that gap.

The district has framed the measure as a choice to act before repairs become emergencies. "We go from a proactive mode to reactive mode," Elsasser said. "We're a day away from a $1 million plumbing problem." Board member Cheryl Fiello said she would "hate to have not taken this opportunity" to ask voters, "and let them decide if that is important to them."
