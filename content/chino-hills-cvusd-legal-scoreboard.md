On Aug. 19, lawyers for Chino Valley Unified will ask a San Bernardino Superior Court judge to dissolve the injunction that has barred the district's parental-notification policy — blocked by courts since 2023, permanently since 2024. How the judge rules will turn on how the pieces of a three-year legal record fit together: the state injunction itself, a labor-board decision, a U.S. Supreme Court emergency order in a different case, and a new statewide law that is often mistaken for part of this fight.

The state injunction. After the California attorney general sued in 2023, a San Bernardino Superior Court judge blocked and then permanently enjoined the policy — numbered 5020.1 — which required schools to tell parents when a student asked to be treated as a gender different from what their official records list. The court found the policy violated California's equal-protection guarantee, Education Code section 220 and Government Code section 11135, and its ruling bars the board from reenacting the original policy. That injunction is in force today, and it is the order the district now wants dissolved.

The labor board. In April, the state Public Employment Relations Board issued a precedential decision finding the district violated California's collective-bargaining law for school employees four ways: it adopted flag-display rules broad enough to be read as banning union insignia; it adopted those rules without giving Associated Chino Teachers notice and a chance to bargain; it adopted the parental-notification policy the same unilateral way; and when it later replaced that policy with a revised version, it adopted portions of that unilaterally too. The decision, No. 3017E, is final at the board level, though the district can still seek review in court.

The U.S. Supreme Court. This is the most easily misread ruling in the record. *Mirabelli v. Bonta* is not a Chino Valley case: two Escondido teachers, later joined by parents, sued their own district's officials and state officials in federal court. A trial judge permanently enjoined state policies that bar schools from disclosing a student's gender identity to parents without the student's consent, and barred schools from overriding a parent's direction on a child's name and pronouns. The Ninth Circuit put that injunction on hold during the appeal. On March 2, the Supreme Court lifted the hold — but only for the parents, leaving it in place for the teachers. The unsigned order said the parents are "likely to succeed on the merits of their Free Exercise Clause claim"; three justices dissented from the part granting the parents relief. It is an emergency-docket order about what happens while the case is litigated, not a final ruling that parents have won.

The collision the district alleges. Its motion argues those orders cannot coexist. The district's lawyers at Advocates for Faith & Freedom and the California Justice Center say Chino Valley now sits under "two conflicting permanent injunctions" — the *Mirabelli* injunction, as they read it, requiring notification, and the 2024 state injunction prohibiting it. Whether *Mirabelli*'s injunction actually reaches Chino Valley is the district's characterization, not a settled fact; that is among the questions the Aug. 19 hearing exists to sort out. The hearing date comes from the district's lawyers, who announced the motion May 6.

The bathroom law. SB 760, which took effect July 1, requires school districts, county offices of education and charter schools serving grades 1-12 to provide at least one all-gender student restroom — signed, unlocked, open during school hours, voluntary to use — at every school that has more than one boys' and more than one girls' restroom. It is a statewide law enforced through state compliance review and conditions on school-construction funding, not a court order. It applies to every district in the state and is not aimed at Chino Valley.
