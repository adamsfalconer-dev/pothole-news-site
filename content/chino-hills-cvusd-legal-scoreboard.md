> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents the legal record standing as of summer 2026.*

Few school districts in California generate as much legal paper — or as much heat — as Chino Valley Unified, which serves Chino and Chino Hills. The fights are real, but they arrive to most readers pre-spun by one side or another. So set the framing aside and read the record: here is what each court and agency has actually done.

**The state injunction (2024).** A San Bernardino Superior Court barred the district's parental-notification policy, known as 5020.1, after the California Attorney General sued. The court found the policy violated the state's equal-protection clause and two state statutes. That injunction is still in force.

**The labor board (April 2026).** In a precedential decision, the state Public Employment Relations Board found the district violated California's labor law twice: with a flag-display policy that swept in the teachers' union's own insignia, and by adopting both the flag policy and the parental-notification policy without first bargaining with Associated Chino Teachers. That is a finding against the district, and it is final at the board level.

**The U.S. Supreme Court (March 2026).** Here is the fact that gets stretched the most. In *Mirabelli v. Bonta*, the Supreme Court issued an emergency order — on its fast-track "shadow docket," by a 6–3 vote — reinstating a lower-court injunction and saying parents are "likely" to have a federal right to be notified. **Why it matters:** an emergency order is not a final ruling on the merits. It signals how the Court is leaning, and it changes the litigation landscape, but it does not itself decide the case. Anyone describing it as a settled "Supreme Court win" is telling you less than the order does.

Those three threads now collide. Citing the Supreme Court's interim order, the district has moved to dissolve the 2024 state injunction, arguing it is caught between conflicting federal and state commands — a "dueling injunctions" problem. That characterization comes from the district's own side; the filing itself is one we have not independently reviewed, and a hearing is reported for August 19. It is the date to watch.

**The new bathroom law (July 2026).** Finally, a piece often folded into the Chino Valley story that doesn't belong to it: SB 760, a statewide law requiring every California district to provide at least one all-gender restroom, took effect July 1. It applies everywhere, enforced by tying school-construction funds to compliance — not by a fine, and not by any order aimed at Chino Valley specifically.

One thing we've left out on purpose. A national group launched an attack website in June built around a board member's statewide candidacy. That is campaign material, and this is a legal scoreboard; the two don't mix.
