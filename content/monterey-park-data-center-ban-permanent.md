> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents events of June–July 2026.*

On June 2, Monterey Park voters did something no California city council can easily undo: they banned data centers, citywide, and locked the ban into the city's General Plan so that it "continues until ended by voters."

The margin was not close. Of 12,381 ballots cast in the special election, about 88% backed the prohibition — a landslide the City Council formally certified on July 1, writing the measure into law as Ordinance 2280.

**Why it matters:** Cities routinely pass moratoriums that a later council can quietly let expire. Monterey Park's voters did the opposite — they took the decision away from any future council. That is what makes a roughly 25,000-household city on the west edge of the San Gabriel Valley a national test case, and it is why the ban drew coverage well beyond California: it is widely described as the first time American voters have used the ballot box to permanently block data centers, not just pause them.

What the measure actually does is narrow and durable. It amends the General Plan's land-use element and the municipal code to prohibit data centers anywhere in the city. Because the prohibition lives in a voter-adopted plan, the council cannot rescind it on a 3–2 night; reversing it requires going back to the ballot. The stated rationale, written into the question voters approved, is protecting air quality, drinking-water resources and public health, and shielding residents from data centers' pull on electricity and water rates.

The vote did not come from nowhere. The council spent the winter building toward it — stacking short-term moratoriums (the city's ordinance trail runs through half a dozen numbers) and then, on March 4, unanimously referring the permanent question to voters while extending the interim ban into January 2027. Residents organized under the banner of a No Data Centers coalition that has since turned up in other valley fights.

One reason the issue resonated is that the industry's local footprint is hard to see. As CalMatters reported in May, data-center operators in California are not required to disclose how much water they consume — so a community weighing one is often arguing over impacts it cannot independently measure. Monterey Park's answer to that uncertainty was to remove the question entirely.
