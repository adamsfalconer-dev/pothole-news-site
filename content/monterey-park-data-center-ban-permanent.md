Monterey Park's ban on data centers took effect Saturday, July 11. The city council that wrote it cannot repeal it.

What voters approved on June 2 — a measure the city titled the Community Act Prohibiting Data Centers — added a prohibition to Monterey Park's Land Use and Urban Design Element: "All data centers are prohibited throughout the City." Data-processing facilities on less than an acre that are accessory to another primary use are exempt. The prohibition itself was not new. The address was.

The council put the question to voters itself. By resolution it placed the proposition on the June 2 ballot, and all five of its members signed the ballot argument urging a yes vote.

It had also banned data centers on its own. The council stacked short-term moratoriums through the winter and, on May 6, adopted a citywide prohibition set to take effect a month later. That was the council's own ordinance, and a council that adopts an ordinance can repeal it on any Wednesday night it chooses.

The land-use element is a different kind of document. Monterey Park's voters adopted it themselves in November 2020, and under the state Elections Code an ordinance the voters adopt cannot be repealed or amended except by another vote of the people — unless the measure itself says otherwise. This one doesn't: it lets the council write the zoning rules that carry the ban out, not undo it. The June measure inserted the data-center prohibition into that element. A future council that wanted to let a data center in would have to ask the voters first.

State planning law carries the rest. Zoning has to be consistent with the general plan; when a plan amendment leaves the zoning inconsistent, the Government Code requires that the zoning be brought back into line, and it lets any resident or property owner go to court to enforce that. So the sentence voters added does not sit inertly in a planning document. It reaches the zoning.

The margin was not close. The county registrar's certified canvass records 10,388 votes for the measure and 1,372 against — 88.33% of the 11,760 ballots that recorded a choice. Not every ballot did: 12,381 were cast in all, and 621 left the question blank. Turnout was 37.77% of the city's 32,781 registered voters. The city's special municipal election was held at the June 2 statewide primary, and the council certified the result on July 1.

Nobody filed an argument against it; the registrar's record of the contest lists no opponents' argument at all.

The city's only data-center applicant was already gone. HMC StratCap 1977 Saturn, LLC, a subsidiary of DigiCo Infrastructure REIT, withdrew its project at 1977 Saturn Street on March 31, telling the council in a letter that it "does not intend to contest the forthcoming ballot proposition."
