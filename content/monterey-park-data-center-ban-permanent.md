The data-center ban Monterey Park voters approved on June 2 took effect Saturday, July 11 — written into the city's General Plan, where no City Council, now or later, can repeal or amend it. Undoing it would take another public vote.

The result was lopsided. The certified count, declared by the council on July 1, was 10,388 to 1,372 — 88.3% of the 11,760 residents who voted on Measure NDC, which ran alongside the June 2 statewide primary. Another 621 of the 12,381 city ballots cast did not record a vote on the measure.

What voters adopted is short. Ordinance 2280 adds one rule to the land-use element of the General Plan: "All data centers are prohibited throughout the City." The measure defines a data center as a facility of networked computer systems on at least an acre of land, counting its cooling equipment, generators and substations. Smaller data-processing facilities — on less than an acre, and accessory or incidental to another primary use — fall outside the definition.

The lock comes from where the ban now lives. Monterey Park's land-use element is itself voter-adopted — approved as Measure JJ in November 2020 — and under the state Elections Code, an ordinance adopted by the voters cannot be "repealed or amended except by a vote of the people, unless provision is otherwise made in the original ordinance." Measure NDC makes no such provision: it lets the council write the zoning rules that carry the ban out, not change it. The city attorney said as much in a March letter, before the vote: the element "may only be amended by voters."

State planning law then drags the rest of the rulebook along. Under Government Code section 65860, a city's zoning must be consistent with its general plan, and when a plan amendment creates a conflict, the zoning "shall be amended within a reasonable time." Any resident or property owner can sue to enforce that — and the section applies to charter cities too.

None of this was done to a reluctant council. The council put the measure on the ballot itself, on March 4, and all five members signed the ballot argument in favor; no argument against was filed. By election day the council had already banned data centers on its own — an urgency citywide prohibition in April, then a permanent ordinance adopted May 6 that took effect June 5. What the June vote changed is who can reverse the ban: an ordinance the council adopts, a later council can repeal; the voter-adopted version, only the voters can.

The industry side of the fight ended before the vote. The only data-center application in the city — from HMC StratCap 1977 Saturn, LLC, a subsidiary of DigiCo Infrastructure REIT, for a project at 1977 Saturn Street — was withdrawn on March 31. The company's letter told the city it "does not intend to contest the forthcoming ballot proposition."

The ban now stands as the reference point for a fight moving through the region. In April, the Los Angeles County Board of Supervisors ordered a county study of data centers' effects on neighboring communities, electricity and water, assigning the findings to its public health and planning departments with five other county offices consulted. A written report from all seven is due back by about Aug. 12 — including a review of how other jurisdictions around the county are regulating data centers. And in Monterey Park, the same voter-lock mechanism is already shaping the city's next land-use question: a July 15 council agenda item sought direction on housing changes to the same General Plan element — changes that, under Measure JJ's voter-approval requirement, could themselves go to the November ballot.
