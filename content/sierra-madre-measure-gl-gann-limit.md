> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents June 2026 events.*

Sierra Madre has cleared the way to keep spending the tax money it already collects — without adding a cent in new taxes. On June 2, voters approved Measure GL by a wide margin, 3,446 to 1,188, or about 74% to 26%, according to the Los Angeles County registrar's certified results.

The measure is not a tax, and it changes no tax rate — a point the city wrote into the measure's official name, the "No-Tax Increase Gann Measure." What it lifts is a ceiling on spending.

**Why it matters:** Under Article XIII B of the California Constitution — the Gann limit, which state voters adopted in 1979 — every city faces an annual cap on how much tax revenue it can actually appropriate, or spend. The cap rises and falls each year with population and inflation. A city can be fully entitled to the taxes it collects and still bump against the limit on how much of that money it is allowed to spend. When that happens, it must either hand the excess back or ask voters to raise the limit — which they can do for up to four years at a time.

Sierra Madre says it is heading toward that ceiling. The city puts its appropriations limit for the current fiscal year at about $14.6 million and forecasts that its spending will pass the cap within four years. Measure GL raises the limit through June 2030, so the revenue the city already collects can keep paying for services rather than being frozen by the cap.

The ballot spelled out where that money goes: clean and safe water, brush clearing and wildfire prevention, firefighter and paramedic staffing, 911 emergency response, street, sidewalk and pothole repair, storm drains and disaster preparedness.

Because the measure raises a spending limit rather than a tax, it needed only a simple majority — a bar it cleared easily, with roughly three of every four voters in favor. The City Council voted unanimously on Feb. 24 to place it on the June 2 ballot, ColoradoBoulevard reported.

For residents, the practical effect is subtle but real: the same taxes, collected at the same rates, but no longer at risk of hitting a spending wall the city says it would otherwise reach before the decade is out.
