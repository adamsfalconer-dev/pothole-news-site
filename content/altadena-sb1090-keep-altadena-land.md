> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents July 2026 events.*

In the year and a half since the Eaton Fire leveled much of Altadena, residents have watched a second wave move through the ashes: investors making offers on burned lots, betting that California's housing laws will let them carve extra parcels out of the neighborhood's deep, single-family lots. A bill now moving through the Legislature aims to slow that down. It has advanced a long way. It is not law.

Here is a plain-language guide to Senate Bill 1090 — the "Keep Altadena Land in Altadena Hands Act" — and exactly where it stands as of mid-July.

**What it would do.** California's SB 9 (2021) and SB 1123 (2024) require local governments to approve certain lot splits and small housing projects "ministerially" — meaning by right, on staff sign-off, with no public hearing and little discretion to say no. SB 1090 would suspend Los Angeles County's obligation to approve those projects ministerially, but only inside unincorporated Altadena, defined by ZIP codes 91001 and 91003. In practice, it would hand the county back the power to review — and potentially reject — speculative splits there.

**What it wouldn't do.** It is not a building ban, and it is not retroactive. The pause would run from Jan. 1, 2027 to Jan. 7, 2030, and it looks forward: projects already filed since the fire are grandfathered in. Owners rebuilding their own homes are not the target; the mechanism is aimed at the by-right splitting of lots, not ordinary reconstruction.

**Why the fire changed the math.** The scale of loss is what makes lot-splitting attractive. CalMatters' Ben Christopher reported that of 5,645 Altadena parcels damaged or destroyed, just 52 currently carry active SB 9 permits — a small number that backers fear is a leading edge rather than the whole story, given how many lots remain empty and for sale.

**Who is behind it.** The author is state Sen. Sasha Renée Pérez, a Pasadena Democrat whose district includes Altadena. Assemblymember John Harabedian is a coauthor, and Los Angeles County Supervisor Kathryn Barger is the sponsor.

**Where it stands — the part to get right.** SB 1090 has cleared several hurdles but not the finish line. The state Senate passed it 30–9 on May 27. The Assembly Housing Committee approved it 11–1 on July 1, and the Assembly Local Government Committee followed 10–0 on July 2. That is where it sits: through two Assembly committees, but not yet passed by the full Assembly, not enrolled, and not signed by the governor. Anyone who tells you it is already law is ahead of the record.
