Chino's draft environmental study for rebuilding Euclid Avenue over Chino Creek is open for public comment until Thursday, July 23 — and the section that answers one of the questions the study records the public asking at its scoping meetings is headed with a different city's project on all ten of its pages.

The Euclid Avenue Bridge Project would carry Euclid over the Prado Basin on a viaduct about 2,500 feet long, widened to four lanes with bicycle and pedestrian lanes, on a project site of about 24.2 acres inside the existing right-of-way. The road it would replace floods. The segment between Pine Avenue and State Route 71 has "been shut down due to flooding on at least five occasions since January 2017," the study says, on a road carrying about 32,500 vehicles a day. The existing bridge "does not have sufficient hydraulic capacity to accommodate a 100-year storm event without overtopping the Bridge."

The replacement would not clear the 100-year mark either. It is built to sit above the basin's 50-year inundation pool — 543.41 feet, a level the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers requires the roadway to clear. Chino opened the review with a notice in January and held two scoping meetings, and three written comments came back. The questions the study records the public asking at those meetings were how long construction would last, what closures to expect, whether 18-wheelers could get through, whether there is federal funding for the work — and "why is the proposed bridge not above the 100-year floodplain."

Section 7.5 is where that question is answered. It studies Alternative 3, a viaduct clearing the 100-year elevation: 38 feet above ground instead of 28, and more than twice as long, about 5,900 feet against 2,500. The study grants that it would do better at the job the project exists to do. Its impacts on hydrology and water quality, public services and transportation "would be less than the Project because all four lanes would be elevated above the 100-year flood elevation, reducing flood-related closures and improving all-weather access." Creating an "all weather" bridge "that remains open and safe during the rainy season" is the project's first stated objective.

Chino set the taller bridge aside, finding its longer, heavier footprint would mean greater impacts on views, air quality, biology, cultural and tribal resources, energy and hazards, and that neither it nor a two-lane version is "clearly environmentally superior." The only alternative the study calls environmentally superior is building nothing.

Every page of that section — printed pages 7.0-18 through 7.0-27 — is headed "City of Riverside" and "Mission Grove Apartments Project DEIR." The pages before it and after it are headed correctly, for Chino; the ten that carry another city's name are exactly the ten that make up Section 7.5. The analysis under the headers is Chino's own: it works through Chino Creek, the Prado Basin, the entrance to Prado Regional Park, the city's municipal code. The header is what belongs somewhere else. Ruth Villalobos & Associates, the firm the study names as its preparer, also prepared the environmental study for the Mission Grove Apartments, a 9.92-acre apartment project in Riverside.

The same section disagrees with the rest of the study about the number the question turns on. "The 100-year flood elevation for the Prado Basin is 533 feet, which is 10 feet higher than the 50-year flood elevation," Section 7.5 says. The study puts the 50-year elevation at 543 feet — in its summary, in its project description, and in its hydrology section, where the Army Corps requires the roadway to clear 543.41 feet. Set against that, the section's 533 puts the 100-year flood ten feet *below* the 50-year flood: the rarer, bigger storm sitting lower than the smaller one.

Neither half of the sentence survives. A 100-year level 10 feet above 543 would be 553, not 533. A 100-year level of 533 would put the 50-year at 523, a figure that appears nowhere in the 425 pages.

There is an innocent reading, and the document supplies it. The hydrology section prints a 553 — "the 100-year water surface impoundment elevation is approximately elevation 553," read off the Army Corps' filling-frequency curves for a dam improved in 2022, though the study brackets those figures as "not a part of the design criteria for the Project." A single digit separates 553 from 533, which points at a misprint rather than a different calculation. The study does not say, and 533 appears exactly once in it: in the section a resident would read to find out why the bridge is not being built higher.

On the merits, the study clears the project. It finds no significant and unavoidable impacts — every effect either minor or reducible by mitigation — and says that a statement of overriding considerations, the finding a council must adopt to approve a project whose harms cannot be fixed, "is not required." Construction is expected to run 18 to 24 months and to begin in 2027.

## How to comment, and by when

Comments go to Michele Hindersinn, senior engineer, City of Chino Public Works Department, 13220 Central Ave., Chino, CA 91710, or to Euclid@CityofChino.org, by Thursday, July 23. Email and mail are the unambiguous routes, and a comment on either is a comment filed.

The city also takes comments on an online form, which it links from its project page at cityofchino.org/Euclid — the surest way to reach it, because the form's own address is easy to mistype. It reads sur-vey.typeform.com/EuclidBridge, with a hyphen inside the word "survey." The address without the hyphen belongs to the form vendor and goes to its marketing pages, where no comment can be filed. The city lists a project helpline at 909-264-8860.

Neither the city's project page nor the state's listing gives a time of day the window closes, so a comment filed earlier in the day on July 23 is the safe one.
