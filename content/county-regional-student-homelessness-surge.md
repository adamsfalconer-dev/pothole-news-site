> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents April 2026 events.*

In a single school year, 2023–24, Los Angeles County public schools counted 61,249 students who experienced homelessness. That is up about 30% from the year before — nearly double California's statewide increase of 16%.

The figure comes from two research briefs released around April 9 by the UCLA Center for the Transformation of Schools. It is the kind of number that is easy to read past and hard to hold: one county, one year, more than sixty thousand children without stable housing while trying to stay in school.

**Why it matters for the San Gabriel Valley:** The county total is not something that happens somewhere else — the SGV's districts are inside it, and its students are among the counted. Student homelessness is measured at the school, by the school, which makes local districts both the place the crisis surfaces and the front line for answering it.

Here is what the researchers found in the population they counted. About 75% of the homeless students were Latino. Thirty-four percent were English learners. And 37.2% were chronically absent — missing so much school that their education is at risk — a figure that shows how directly unstable housing turns into empty desks.

The gap between districts is stark. The highest-rate districts, UCLA found, ran two to six times the state average. This does not spread evenly; it concentrates, and some communities carry far more of it than others.

Which San Gabriel Valley districts sit at the high end is exactly the question a local reader wants answered — and it is the one we are being careful about. Some secondary coverage of the briefs named specific SGV districts among the highest-rate, including Rowland Unified, which serves Rowland Heights, and Mountain View Elementary in El Monte. But we could not confirm that ranking against UCLA's published briefs, and the secondary list also included a district name that does not correspond to any real San Gabriel Valley district. Rowland Unified and Mountain View Elementary are real districts worth watching here; the specific ranking is not something we will print as settled until it checks against the source.

Why did Los Angeles County's rise so outpace the state's? That is the question the numbers raise and do not, on their own, answer. The briefs document the increase; the causes behind a 30% county jump against a 16% state one are the reporting still to be done.
