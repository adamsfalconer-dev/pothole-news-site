Armstrong Elementary School is gone. Pomona Unified's board voted 4–1 on May 13 to consolidate it into Golden Springs Elementary, and the closure took effect June 30. Armstrong's principal will run Golden Springs, which opens Aug. 12. The school's page on the district's website returns an empty document, and Armstrong does not appear anywhere on the district's school directory — every other school does. The Daily Bulletin's Allyson Vergara first reported the vote and the campus's reuse in May.

The board acted on an equity impact analysis it approved the same night, on the same 4–1 split. It was prepared by Total School Solutions, a private consulting firm, and presented to the board by two of its consultants. State law requires districts in financial distress to complete that analysis before deciding to close a school. This one does not add up.

Its headline savings figure is $591,000 a year. But $147,000 of that — utilities — is available, the analysis says, only "if the site were leased rather than repurposed by the District." Resolution 30, which the board passed two items later the same night, repurposes the site: Armstrong became the "Armstrong Educational Center," housing the district's Learning Enrichment program, effective July 1. The analysis separately warns that the $20,000 in child-nutrition savings may turn out to be "very little or no." The only component it neither conditions nor hedges is $424,000 — the general-fund figure, about seven-tenths of the number that led the case for closing the school. What the utilities are worth if the district occupies the campus itself, the analysis never estimates; how a campus is reused, it says, "will determine how much utility savings can be achieved."

The analysis also reports 281 surplus seats at Golden Springs. Its own numbers do not produce that. It gives Armstrong 215 students and Golden Springs 418 — 633 together — against a Golden Springs capacity of 870. That leaves 237. The document does not explain the other 44. One table earlier it performs the same subtraction correctly, putting Golden Springs' excess at 452, which is 870 minus 418.

And the row reporting 281 is impossible on its own terms. It also states the combined enrollment fills 72% of capacity. Seats in use plus seats empty cannot add up to more than the seats that exist — but 281 empty seats is another 32% of 870, for 104%.

On the school's condition, the analysis carries two ratings it never reconciles. Section 2.2 reports a facility condition index of 65, "indicating a poor rating for Armstrong Elementary School." Section 2.4.1 says the district's facilities master plan "rates Armstrong with an FCI of 'Good.'" Both describe the same campus, which the same document prices at $7.5 million to renovate.

The district's stated reason for the consolidation is enrollment. "The District has experienced declining enrollment for the past several years making it necessary to consolidate student population in order to maintain responsible stewardship of resources," the rationale for the consolidation item reads. At the May 13 meeting, five parents spoke against the consolidation during public comment. The minutes record no speaker in support.

Superintendent Darren Knowles defended the merger publicly. "These decisions are never easy because schools are more than buildings; they are places where students, families and staff create lasting memories and connections," he told the Daily Bulletin in May. "By bringing students together and maximizing available resources, we believe this transition will help strengthen programs, expand opportunities and ensure we continue supporting students both now and into the future."

Knowles's statement addressed the consolidation rather than the analysis. The district's website says nothing about the document's discrepancies, and no Armstrong item appears on the board's June 24 or July 15 agendas.

Armstrong sent a higher share of students with disabilities than the school receiving them — 15.5% against 9.0% — and more homeless students, 5.2% against 3.9%. The analysis quotes the state attorney general's caution against closure criteria that penalize "school sites with special day classes." It does not apply that caution to Armstrong.

The minutes list the topics the board raised before voting — "staffing, vacancies, class rosters, buildings at Golden Springs Elementary School, student population, and student transfers" — and record that Knowles "and Cabinet Members provided detailed answers and additional information for each question." They do not record what those answers were.

Trustee Tamara Gonzalez cast the only vote against the consolidation and against the equity analysis. Trustees Nashua, Perlman, Jimenez and Tye voted for both. On a third measure — a finding that the closure was exempt from environmental review — Gonzalez abstained and the rest voted yes.
