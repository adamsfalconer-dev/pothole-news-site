Monterey Park residents who bought an air purifier while smoke from the Boyle Heights warehouse fire hung over the city can claim part of the cost back. They have until July 31, and the city says the money may not last that long.

The Air Purifier Reimbursement Program pays up to $75, or 40 percent of the purchase price, whichever amount is less. The purifier had to have been bought between June 17 and June 28. One reimbursement per household. "Funding is limited and will be distributed on a first-come, first-served basis," the city says.

The program is income-qualified, and the ceiling rises with household size: $43,280 for a household of one or two, $54,640 for three, $66,000 for four, $77,360 for five, $88,720 for six, and $11,360 more for each additional person.

An application needs the city's form plus proof of residency, proof of income eligibility, and the original purchase receipt. Residents can file at the Water Counter at City Hall, 320 W. Newmark Ave., or by email to utilitypayments@montereypark.ca.gov. One wrinkle worth knowing: the Water Counter is open Monday through Thursday, so the last day to file in person is Thursday, July 30. Email still works on the 31st.

The purchase window closed weeks ago, so who is eligible is already settled. What isn't settled is whether the people who spent the money file for it before the deadline — or before the fund empties.

The fire was never Monterey Park's. It began June 17 at a warehouse at 1400 S. Los Palos Street in Boyle Heights — the street the fire department named the incident for — and burned about a week, with knockdown declared June 25. The smoke came east. On June 22 the city canceled all outdoor recreation programs through June 28, among them a June 25 watch party and farmers' market and the Independence Day Celebration set for Saturday, June 27 at Barnes Park. It gave out N-95 masks that evening at fire stations until 8, two per household with proof of residency, for residents with health sensitivities and while supplies lasted, and opened the Langley Center and Bruggemeyer Library as relief sites the same day.

"Protecting the health and well-being of our residents remains a top priority," then-Mayor Elizabeth Yang said in the city's June 25 release announcing the reimbursement. "We recognize that many families incurred unexpected expenses to safeguard indoor air quality during the recent fire."
