Six Pasadena Unified schools that a consultant's report had marked for possible closure or merger will stay open — and two of the trustees who set that process in motion are now the targets of a recall.

At a meeting on May 28 that ran more than seven hours, the board voted 6-1 to formally reject the equity analysis — an outside study of possible school consolidations — with Fredericks casting the lone vote against rejecting it, according to the board's minutes. The $230,000 analysis had placed six schools — Blair, Thurgood Marshall, Don Benito, McKinley, Webster and Norma Coombs — on a potential closure or merger list, according to Pasadena Now, which covered the meeting; the district's legal advisor told trustees that any future closure effort would have to begin again with a new study, and no school is closing under the halted process. Two weeks later, at a June 11 special session, the board moved to end its contract with the consultant, Total School Solutions, which the outlet reported had been hired for up to $233,300.

The same night the plan was halted, recall organizers delivered formal notices of intent to circulate recall petitions to two board members: president Tina Fredericks, who represents Trustee Area 6, and Scott Harden, of Area 4. The board's minutes record Dawn Denison announcing the intent to recall Fredericks and Marisol Tansey serving the recall notice on Harden.

The recall's stated grounds are about how the consolidation was set in motion, not the vote that stopped it. The petition accuses the trustees of violating California's Ralph M. Brown Act — the state's open-meetings law — by coordinating consolidation plans through private, serial communications, and says Fredericks drafted and advanced her own "Consolidation 2027" plan while portraying the district's process as independent. It alleges she worked with the consultant before the firm received its taxpayer-funded contract, and that the trustees "betrayed the trust of the voters." Those are the petitioners' allegations; no court or agency has ruled on them.

The district says its lawyers looked and found nothing unlawful. In a statement authorized at the June 11 closed session, Pasadena Unified said its legal counsel had reviewed the allegations and concluded the board did not violate the Brown Act: with a seven-member board, "up to three board members may engage in conversation outside of a public meeting," the district said, and "the evidence does not support an allegation that four or more board members exchanged comments/positions." The allegations trace in part to a batch of the trustees' own emails and texts, released under a records request and read aloud at the May meeting — including a message a speaker attributed to the consulting firm's chief executive telling several trustees it "would be best to not mention our discussions to the superintendent and let her own the process."

The trustees have answered the criticism in public. Harden apologized for what he called "sarcastic and cynical" text messages but said he attended a single consultant presentation and made "no effort to endorse them, recommend their hire to anyone, direct their work, or conspire to achieve any specific result." Trustee Kimberly Kenne, who also voted for the enrollment resolution at the center of a related open-meetings complaint, said she regretted her "sarcastic tone" and had no contact with the firm before its January presentation. Fredericks has said her earlier consolidation materials were "personal notes" the firm never reviewed or contributed to, and has described her actions as lawful due diligence in the face of the district's finances.

On July 2, Fredericks took the fight to court. Representing herself, she filed a verified petition for a writ of mandate and for injunctive relief in Los Angeles Superior Court — case No. 26STCP02505 — naming the county registrar-recorder and 124 unidentified "Doe" respondents, the number of District 6 residents who signed the notice of intent to recall her. The petition asks a judge to bar the county from letting the recall against her circulate, arguing, as reported by Pasadena Now, that it contains "false and misleading statements" about her role and that only "two or three of the seven" members were ever involved, below the threshold for an unlawful serial meeting. She has denied the allegations in the recall.

The court's docket shows no judge has ruled on that request. It records the July 2 petition, the case's July 6 assignment to Judge Tiana J. Murillo in Department 834, and a single scheduled event — a trial-setting conference on September 8. No temporary restraining order has been entered, and the county had not filed a response. Harden's recall is not part of Fredericks's lawsuit.

The recall has drawn public sides. Sierra Madre Mayor Kristine Lowe has called on Fredericks to resign and spoken in favor of the recall. The United Teachers of Pasadena, the district's teachers union, came out against it, saying "a recall at this time is not the way" and that "an off-year recall election diverts even more funding away from our students." Pasadena Unified serves Pasadena, Altadena and Sierra Madre.

Underneath all of it is money. The district has projected a budget shortfall of $30 million to $35 million for the coming year — a gap it has warned could widen to $71 million by 2027-28 — driven by falling enrollment, the end of one-time pandemic aid and damage from the January 2025 Eaton Fire, which destroyed or damaged five district schools in Altadena. In February, the board approved cutting more than 412 positions. The pressures that produced a school-closure list have not gone away; only this particular list has.
