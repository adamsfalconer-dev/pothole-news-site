Chino Hills residents could soon be asked to tax themselves. On Tuesday, the City Council decides whether to place a 1-cent sales tax on the November 3 ballot — a penny more on every dollar spent in the city.

The number the city is chasing is $11.5 million a year. That is roughly $144 for every resident, and it is the city's answer to a budget that no longer balances: staff project $59.2 million in revenue against $63.5 million in spending this fiscal year, a $4.3 million gap the city is filling from savings.

**Why it matters:** Chino Hills has long marketed itself as a low-tax, well-run suburb. A sales-tax measure is an admission that the model is straining — and it hands voters, not the council, the decision. If it passes, nearly every purchase in the city costs more; if it fails, the council says the math gets harder fast.

City Manager Rod Hill has told the council the city holds $25.4 million in unrestricted reserves — a real cushion — but that those funds empty within three to five years if current trends hold. That reserve figure is the tension in Tuesday's vote: healthy enough to question the urgency, shrinking fast enough to explain it.

The council laid the groundwork in June, adopting the 2026-27 budget on a 4-0 vote and directing staff to bring back revenue options — including the sales tax — at its first July meeting. The Champion's Marianne Napoles first reported the measure's trajectory on June 13, noting Councilman Ray Marquez's support; the San Bernardino County Sentinel has corroborated the July-deadline timeline. Tuesday is the formal step: a ballot-resolution adoption, not a surprise.

If the council votes yes, the measure needs a simple majority of Chino Hills voters in November to take effect.
