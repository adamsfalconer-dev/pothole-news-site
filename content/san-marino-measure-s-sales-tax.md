> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents June 2026 events.*

San Marino shoppers will now pay an extra penny on every dollar at the register. On June 2, voters approved Measure S, a 1% sales tax, by a wide margin — 2,545 votes yes to 1,381 no, or about 65% to 35%, according to the Los Angeles County registrar's certified results.

The measure raises the city's combined sales-tax rate from 9.75% to 10.75% and is expected to bring in roughly $1.65 million a year. That is enough, on paper, to cover both the deficit the city is running now and the larger one it says is coming.

**Why it matters:** San Marino says it is spending more than it takes in, and the gap is widening. City figures put the current structural deficit at about $787,867 and project it growing toward $1.25 million a year within five years, against some $46.8 million in unfunded capital needs — aging streets, sidewalks and storm drains the general fund can't keep up with. Measure S is the city's chosen fix.

How the money can be used is the part worth reading closely. Measure S is a *general* tax, which under state law means it needed only a simple majority to pass — which it cleared easily — but also that the revenue flows into the city's general fund for any lawful purpose. The city's list of intended uses — street, sidewalk and stormwater repair, public safety, parks and the library, and youth and senior programs — reflects the council's stated priorities, not a binding legal earmark. A tax legally dedicated to those specific uses would have needed a two-thirds vote.

Getting the measure on a June ballot at all required an unusual step. Because no City Council seats were up for election, state law barred the city from asking voters for a general tax unless the council first unanimously declared a fiscal emergency. It did so on Feb. 11 with Resolution R-26-04, Outlook Newspapers reported — the legal key that unlocked the June 2 special election.

The result gives San Marino a new, recurring revenue stream that grows with local spending, without touching property-tax bills. What it does not do is settle how the council prioritizes the money now that it is unrestricted.
