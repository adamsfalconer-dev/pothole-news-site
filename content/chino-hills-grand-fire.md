> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents May 2026 events.*

For about two hours on the evening of May 13, the foothills above Grand Avenue in Chino Hills, in San Bernardino County, looked like the start of something much worse. A brush fire had ignited near Grand Avenue and Pleasant Hill Drive, by Grand Avenue Park, and by 8:15 p.m. crews were ordering foothill residents below the flames to leave.

By 10:15 that same night, every evacuation order was lifted. No homes burned. One firefighter suffered a minor injury; no civilians were hurt.

The Grand Fire — named, like the park, for the avenue — broke out around 7:13 p.m. and topped out at about 36 acres. The Chino Valley Independent Fire District led the response in unified command with CAL FIRE, with roughly 250 personnel on the line. Containment reached 50 percent by Thursday morning and 75 percent by Thursday night.

**Why it matters:** Chino Hills sits in the kind of terrain — homes pressed against dry, steep open space — where a small ignition can become a catastrophe in minutes. This time it didn't, and the reason is the response: fast evacuation orders for roughly 650 threatened structures, a quick knockdown and orders lifted the same night. A well-handled fire is still news, precisely because the outcome was not guaranteed.

The cause was not lightning or a downed line. A district fire investigator determined that the blaze began with a single-vehicle rollover crash that caught fire and spread into the surrounding vegetation, as ABC7 reported. The fire district shared a photo of the burned, overturned vehicle — a Tesla — that it said started it.

For residents who had to move quickly, the city opened an evacuation center at the Chino Hills Community Center, 14250 Peyton Drive, CBS Los Angeles reporter Dean Fioresi reported. It was a short-lived need: with the orders rescinded before midnight, the emergency was largely over the night it began.
