Pasadena Unified's school board voted Thursday night to declare the City of Pasadena's zoning ordinances inapplicable at 10 of its properties — the rules behind the stop-work orders the city has posted over campus tree removals — and, separately, authorized a lawsuit against a defendant the district declined to name.

Resolution 2896 invokes Government Code section 53094, a state law that lets a school board, by a two-thirds vote of its members, render city zoning rules inapplicable to district property. The resolution declares the city's zoning ordinances "in Title 17 of the City's Municipal Code, and elsewhere" inapplicable at 10 listed sites — nine schools, from Blair High School to Washington Elementary STEM Magnet, plus the district's own Education Center on South Hudson Avenue. Scott Sachs, the district's outside counsel, told trustees the resolution would remove the basis for the city's stop-work notices, Pasadena Now reported.

Two-thirds of the seven-member board is five votes, and by the Star-News' count that is exactly what the resolution got: it passed 5-2, the paper reported, with board President Tina Fredericks and Trustee Scott Harden voting no. The district has not yet posted minutes, so the official tally is not on the record.

The litigation authorization came first, announced as the board returned from closed session. The board "approved initiating litigation to protect against interference" with district campuses and the soil-remediation project, Fredericks announced — a unanimous vote, both outlets reported. Neither she nor district officials said whom the district intends to sue.

The exemption is not the last word. Section 53094 requires the district to notify the city within 10 days — Resolution 2896 directs the superintendent to do exactly that — and the city may then go to Superior Court to challenge the board's action. If a judge finds it "arbitrary and capricious," the law says, the exemption is "of no force and effect" and the zoning ordinance applies again.

The vote is the sharpest turn yet in a standoff that has escalated all month. The district is removing soil contaminated by the Eaton Fire at its campuses under a $6.6 million emergency contract, with the state Department of Toxic Substances Control's written concurrence that affected trees "must also be removed and replaced." The city, whose tree-protection ordinance requires permits to remove mature and protected trees, posted stop-work notices at campuses in early July, and on July 10 sent an arborist onto John Muir High School under a court-issued inspection warrant, Pasadena Now reported. By that week 78 trees were down, officials told LAist. Staff's July 16 presentation to the board counts 87 trees still standing at the project sites, 41 of them potentially mature or native under the city's criteria.

The city answered the vote in writing. The city "has always valued our collaborative relationship and has worked diligently to assist PUSD," spokesperson Lisa Derderian said in a statement, adding that its focus remains "the retention of protected trees during the soil remediation process." The city has been weighing litigation of its own: its council met in closed session July 8 and again July 13 — two potential cases it might initiate, by the July 13 agenda, plus one where it faces "significant exposure" — sessions the Star-News tied to the tree dispute.
