> *Reported July 2026 as part of our founding archive — this account documents spring 2026 events.*

Three quiet foothill towns, three spring burglaries, one uncomfortable thing in common: the people who did them appear to have done their homework, with technology.

This is a pattern, not a proven conspiracy — and the distinction matters, so hold onto it. What links the cases is method, not a single charged crew. Here is what the record shows.

**San Dimas, May 12.** Deputies responding to a burglary on the 800 block of Via Gregorio found something that turned a break-in into a story: concealed surveillance devices staged around the property — a camera wired to a cellular hotspot and a battery, and a cellphone camouflaged to blend in. The Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department believes the cameras were there to time when residents came and went. No one has been arrested.

**La Cañada Flintridge, May 2.** In a burglary on El Vago Drive, intruders sprayed the family's dog with bear spray and used signal jammers to knock out the home's security system before going in. No arrests.

**Sierra Madre, April 24.** On Oak Meadow, burglars forced their way in and ransacked the rooms. No arrests.

The method investigators describe across cases like these is consistent and deliberate: cameras hidden in the landscaping, Wi-Fi and signal jammers to defeat alarms and doorbell cameras, rental cars, burner phones, and advance surveillance of targets through social media.

**Why it matters:** These are exactly the neighborhoods — leafy, well-off, low-crime — that buy the doorbell cameras and alarm systems the method is built to defeat. A jammer does not care how new your camera is. For residents, the unsettling part is not a smash-and-grab; it is the evidence that someone studied the house first.

Here is where precision is a public service. On May 20, Los Angeles County District Attorney Nathan Hochman and Sheriff Robert Luna announced that seven people had been charged in three residential-burglary cases. Those cases were in the San Fernando Valley and West Los Angeles. They are not the San Dimas, La Cañada Flintridge or Sierra Madre cases, which remain unsolved and uncharged. It is tempting to fold a well-timed set of charges into a foothill mystery; the records do not let us, and we will not.

For all of it, some context that cuts against the alarm: according to reported-crime data, residential burglaries countywide have been falling — from 10,419 in 2022 to 8,453 in 2025. A frightening method is not the same as a rising tide.

That leaves the limits of what police can say. In these three cases there are no suspects in custody, no charges, and — importantly — no official label. In the releases on these specific break-ins, investigators have not attached any group name or origin to the people responsible, and neither will we. Everyone involved is unidentified and, if ever arrested, presumed innocent. The method is documented; the who is not.
